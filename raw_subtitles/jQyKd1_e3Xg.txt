Next, let's talk about accuracy for a second. When people talk about AI search accuracy, the headline number sounds very reassuring. A study by the AI startup Umie, conducted for the New York Times, tested 4,326 Google searches and found that AI overviews powered by Gemini 3 were accurate 91 percent of the time. When Google was a link engine, accuracy meant relevance. Did the search return useful websites related to your query? You were the one verifying information, cross-referencing sources, deciding which site to trust. The cognitive labor of evaluation was yours. Now Google gives you the answer, a single, definitive-looking response presented in a clean box at the top of the page, formatted with the visual authority of a settled fact. It's set in stone now. The standard has fundamentally changed. Relevance and correctness are not the same thing, and the product is now being held to a bar it set for itself by changing its own format. 91 percent accuracy for a link engine would be extraordinary. 91 percent accuracy for a system that presents itself as the answer with no obvious signal to the user about when they're in the other 9 percent is a different proposition. And at Google's scale, which is 5 trillion searches annually, that 9 percent translates to roughly 57 million incorrect answers every hour, hundreds of thousands every minute, which is less of a rounding error and more of a public service announcement.
