Yet, despite their immense wealth, a striking disconnect exists: **nobody likes them**. A recent survey by Forbes and Harris X revealed that Americans' views on billionaires are unfavorable, ranking them similarly to members of Congress and barely above TikTok influencers.

### 亿万富翁的经济驱动力

However, this public disdain masks a critical truth: **the world desperately needs billionaires**. Countries with billionaires tend to possess the hallmarks of progress: jobs, growth, and societal advancement. Consider China: at the turn of the century, it had virtually no billionaires on the Forbes list and nearly 500 million people in extreme poverty. Two decades later, the situation is reversed, with almost 500 Chinese billionaires and no one living in extreme poverty – a correlation that is not coincidental. Similarly, Nigeria, despite its large population, has only four billionaires, yet 100 million people live in extreme poverty. The absence of billionaires in countries like Cuba, Iran, and Belarus further illustrates a historical pattern: **incentive creates personal wealth and societal wealth**. When you cap earnings, that correlate with repression and stagnation.

### 财富差距与商业的终极目标

So, why all the hate? A significant factor is **wealth disparity**. With SpaceX reportedly going public soon, Elon Musk may become the world's first trillionaire. While this figure is staggering – a million million – it's crucial to note that Elon's wealth, adjusted for GDP, is not vastly greater than that of John D. Rockefeller when he became the world's first billionaire a century ago. Rockefeller, an OG monopolist, was highly respected by the end of his life due to his significant philanthropic work, as was Andrew Carnegie. People generally aspire to become billionaires, not resent them. Resentment arises when billionaires forget that the fundamental purpose of business is to create happiness, not merely to die with the most toys.

### 慈善赤字与“真实净资产”的诞生

This brings us to the critical **philanthropy deficit**. Currently, the world's five richest people, all Americans with net worths exceeding $200 million, collectively donate less than 1% of their net worth to charity (0.9%). This starkly contrasts with the average American – a teacher or fireman – who gives around 2% of their income. This gap is a primary driver of societal discontent, the very reason for the "pitchforks." Fortunately, this is an addressable issue, one that can be influenced by tapping into a powerful motivator for many billionaires: their egos. The allure of Forbes lists and rankings is undeniable. From the "Trump rule" of wealth estimation to Prince Alwaleed's stock manipulations, Kylie Jenner's alleged inflated company numbers, and Kanye West's demands regarding his perceived wealth, the desire for status is a potent force. Inspired by these dynamics, we've created **True Net Worth**（真实净资产）, a new metric designed to reward rather than penalize.

### 财富榜样与慷慨的意义

True Net Worth combines a person's regular net worth with their charitable donations, recognizing this giving as if they still "own" it. This system offers a new way to measure impact. For instance, **Warren Buffett**, a true pioneer, has already given away more in today's dollars than he still possesses. He has pledged 99% of his remaining wealth to charity, with the stipulation that it must be spent within 10 years of his death, rather than sitting in a foundation for decades. Another prominent example is **MacKenzie Scott**, who is distributing wealth faster, smarter, and with no strings attached, more than almost anyone in history. While she ranks 84th by net worth, she is 26th by True Net Worth. This demonstrates that **people don't value what you can't measure**. True Net Worth offers role models for everyone, from billionaires to millionaires to thousandaires. The message is clear: "Give while you live. Give faster and bigger, perhaps more than you are comfortable with. Donate your money or your time. Ultimately, all of this supports the system that makes all of us prosperous."
