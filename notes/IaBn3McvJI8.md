### 接受有限的控制与深远的影响

这里的核心观点之一是，当你开始接受你对世界有着深远的影响，但控制力却非常有限时，你就会开始以不同的方式看待世界，以不同的方式行事，并开始接受自己能力和局限的边界。

</details>

因此，当我们设想一切事出有因时，这会导致我们犯下**认知错误**（Cognitive Mistakes: 思维上的系统性偏差），也使我们对生活中的事件赋予了根本上错误的意义。

### “事出有因”的幻觉

在我们的一生中，我们被告知自己掌控着人生的道路，我们认为自己是主角，只要做出明智的决定，一切都会好起来。而当事情不如意时，我们总是听到那句话：一切事出有因。

<p class="english-text">Throughout our lives, we are told that we're in control of our path through life, that we have this idea of being the main character, and as long as we make wise decisions, then everything will turn out all right. And when things don't turn out all right, the saying that we always hear is that everything happens for a reason.</p>
</details>

然而，这两者都是不真实的。我们并非掌控一切，事实上，我认为在《**偶然**》（Fluke: 布莱恩·克拉斯所著探讨随机性与控制幻觉的书籍）一书中的一个核心论点是，我们无法控制任何事物，但我们能影响一切。同时，我们所拥有的这种**控制幻觉**（Illusion of Control: 认为自己能影响实际无法控制的事件的认知偏差），以及将一切都归结为有明确原因的叙事方式，导致我们误解世界并犯下错误。

And also, at the same time, this illusion of control that we have and the stitching together of this clear narrative where everything has a reason behind it causes us to misunderstand the world and make mistakes.</p>

### 科学与哲学对随机性的理解

因此，科学证据表明并非所有事情都事出有因。所以，如果有些事情只是随意、随机发生，或者作为**混沌理论**（Chaos Theory: 研究动态系统中对初始条件敏感依赖的复杂行为的数学分支）的副产品，创造了我们所有人都是随机事件的傀儡，那么你需要对你思考世界的方式进行哲学上的改变。

<details>
<summary>View/Hide Original English</summary>
<p class="english-text">And so, the scientific evidence shows us that everything does not happen for a reason, and therefore, you need to have a philosophical change in how you think about the world if some things just happen arbitrarily, randomly, or as the byproduct of chaos theory, creating the puppets that we all are of random events.</p>
</details>

因此，在我看来，世界思想史基本上就是一部试图将世界的复杂性和混乱塞进一个整洁有序的故事中，以解释事物为何发生。这部分源于宗教，人们希望从上帝那里获得优雅的秩序，对吗？现实具有某种偶然性的观念，与一切都由更高存在所计划的观念完全相悖。所以，在整个宗教历史中，总是有这种整洁有序的故事，即“一切事出有因”的方面与神性联系在一起。

<details>
<summary>View/Hide Original English</summary>
<p class="english-text">So, the history of ideas in the world, in my view, is basically a history of trying to cram the complexity and messiness of the world into a really neat and tidy story for why things happen. This is partly derived from religion, where people wanted to have elegant order from God, right? The idea that there's a sort of accidental nature to reality is completely at odds with the notion that everything is planned by a higher being. So, throughout religious history, there's always this neat and tidy story, the 'everything happens for a reason' aspect that's tied to the Divine.</p>
</details>

接着，随着科学的发展和**科学革命**（Scientific Revolution: 16-18世纪欧洲科学思想和实践的根本性转变）的推进，又出现了一个转变。它不再仅仅是关于上帝；它更多是关于这些**发条装置物理模型**（Clockwork Models of Physics: 一种将宇宙视为精密机械般运作的决定论观点），它们非常非常优雅，对吗？而这就是那种你必须消除噪音、消除偶然性的东西，因为一切都应该是一个真正美丽的方程式，无论是**亚当·斯密**（Adam Smith: 苏格兰经济学家和哲学家，被誉为“经济学之父”）谈论那只引导我们经济的手，还是**艾萨克·牛顿**（Isaac Newton: 英国物理学家、数学家、天文学家、自然哲学家和神学家，被誉为“科学之父”）呈现变化的方式以及那些令人难以置信的强大方程来解释世界。我们总是试图找到一个能将一切都纳入有序解释的答案。

<details>
<summary>View/Hide Original English</summary>
<p class="english-text">Then, as science unfolded and the Scientific Revolution developed, you have another shift. It's not necessarily that it's all about God; it's all about these clockwork models of physics, and they're very, very elegant, right? And this is the kind of stuff where you have to eliminate the noise, you have to eliminate the accidents, because everything is supposed to be an equation that is really beautiful, whether it's Adam Smith talking about the hand that guides our economies, or the ways that Isaac Newton presented change and these unbelievably powerful equations to explain the world. We have always tried to find the explanation that fits everything into an explanation that seems ordered.</p>
</details>

### 偶然聚合与模式痴迷的大脑

这就是为什么我认为人们对**偶然聚合**（Contingent Convergence: 指由一系列随机或非必然事件共同导致某一特定结果的现象）这个概念有如此多的抵触，因为它有时是不合逻辑的。你知道，我可以审视我自己的生活，并理解我是大规模谋杀的副产品，或者成千上万的人会因为一对夫妇19年前的一次随机度假而死在一个城市——这些事情并非整洁有序，它们没有秩序，也不优雅，但它们是真实的。我认为科学的特点之一是，许多非常奇怪的事情确实存在，我们最好接受它们，直面这种不确定性、复杂性和随机性，而不是简单地假装它不是这样运作的，因为它在某种程度上能安慰我们那些痴迷于模式的大脑。

<details>
<summary>View/Hide Original English</summary>
<p class="english-text">Now, this is why I think there's so much resistance to this notion of contingent convergence because it is irrational sometimes. You know, the fact that I can look at my own life and understand that I'm the byproduct of a mass murder, or that hundreds of thousands of people will die in one city because of a random vacation a couple took 19 years earlier—these things are not neat and tidy. They're not ordered; they're not elegant, but they're true. And I think one of the things about science is that a lot of really strange things exist, and it's better for us to just accept them and sort of stare into the face of this uncertainty, this complexity, and this randomness, than to simply pretend it doesn't work this way because it's somehow comforting to our pattern-obsessed brains.</p>
</details>

因此，当你审视世界的思想史时，随机性一直被系统性地排除在外，我认为当我们试图理解生活中发生在我们身上的事情时，这是一个错误。

<details>
<summary>View/Hide Original English</summary>
<p class="english-text">So, when you look at the intellectual history of the world, randomness has systematically been written out, and I think it's a mistake when we try to understand why stuff has happened to us in our lives.</p>
</details>

我们的大脑已经进化到能够理解模式，这是因为过度检测模式对生存有利。所以，如果你能想象你是一个史前时代的狩猎采集者，你看到草丛中有一点沙沙声，或者听到一个声音，也许那里什么都没有，也许有一只剑齿虎正等着吃掉你。如果你想象那里什么都没有，结果却是一只剑齿虎，你就会死去。但如果你过度解读模式，并说：“好吧，我想那可能是我需要提防的东西”，即使那里什么都没有，你仍然会活下来。因此，通过**适者生存**（Survival of the Fittest: 达尔文进化论中的核心概念，指在生存竞争中，最适应环境的个体更容易存活和繁殖）和进化模式，我们的大脑对模式检测高度敏感。

<details>
<summary>View/Hide Original English</summary>
<p class="english-text">Our brains have evolved to make sense of patterns, and that's because it's advantageous for survival to over-detect patterns. So, if you can imagine that you're a prehistoric hunter-gatherer and you see a little bit of rustling in the grass or you hear a sound, maybe there's nothing there, or maybe there's a saber-tooth tiger that's waiting to eat you. If you imagine there's nothing there and it turns out to be a saber-tooth tiger, you die. But if you over-interpret the pattern and you say, 'Okay, I think that might be something that I need to watch out for,' even if there's nothing there, then you'll still survive. So, through survival of the fittest and evolutionary patterns, our brains are hyper-attuned to pattern detection.</p>
</details>

这意味着，当随机或看似随机的事情发生在我们身上时，我们对“这只是偶然”的解释感到排斥，因此我们会编织出一个从A到B的整洁有序的故事。

<details>
<summary>View/Hide Original English</summary>
<p class="english-text">Now, this means that when random or seemingly random things happen to us, we're allergic to the explanation that it was just arbitrary, and so we stitch together a neat and tidy story from A to B.</p>
</details>

现在，这是一个问题，因为当发生在你身上的事情是你无法控制的，而你却将其归因于某种意图时，你就误解了其中的教训，对吗？

<details>
<summary>View/Hide Original English</summary>
<p class="english-text">Now, that's a problem because when stuff happens to you that you don't have control over, and you ascribe intentionality to it, you've misread the lesson, right?</p>
</details>

### 过去与现在：因果模式的恒定性

我认为另一个非常引人注目的事情是，当我们思考过去时，我们理解这一点，但当我们思考现在时，我们却不理解。例如，当我们观看想象时间旅行的科幻电影时，人们普遍接受的警告总是：“你不应该触碰任何东西；你不应该与任何人交谈，因为你可能会不小心将自己从未来抹去，或者从根本上改变你回到现在时所要面对的模式。”所以，不要踩死一只虫子；不要与错误的人交谈。问题是，当我们思考现在时，我们从不那样想。我们从不想象，如果我们踩死一只虫子或者与某人交谈，我们正在重塑未来。但当然，因果模式无论在过去还是现在都不会改变；它们始终以相同的方式运作。

<details>
<summary>View/Hide Original English</summary>
<p class="english-text">And I think one of the things that's also very striking is that we understand this when we think about the past, but we don't understand this when we think about the present. So, when we watch Sci-Fi films, for example, that imagine time travel, the warning that is always issued, which people fundamentally accept, is: 'You shouldn't touch anything; you shouldn't talk to anyone, because you might accidentally delete yourself from the future or fundamentally change the patterns that you will then go back to when you return to your present time.' So, don't squish a bug; don't talk to the wrong person. The problem is that when we think in the present, we never think that way. We never imagine that if we squish a bug or if we talk to someone, we're reshaping the future. But of course, cause and effect patterns don't change whether they're in the past or the present; they operate the same all the time.</p>
</details>

因此，我认为我们对待过去的思维方式是正确的：我们正在不断重塑未来，我们所做的事情很重要。而这个想法，我认为我们也有些排斥，因为它太令人难以承受了，想象我们所做的每一个单一行为都具有不可预见的**连锁反应**（Ripple Effects: 指一个事件或行为所引发的一系列间接且广泛的影响），将改变世界并重塑我们的未来。这令人困惑，但它也是真实的。我认为当你仔细审视因果关系、现实和存在的本质时，会发现那些令人困惑的事情一直在发生，而我们只是忽略了它们。我认为当你深入探究它们时，你会开始看到一个非常不同的世界，一个如果你真正接受有些事情并非事出有因，并且事实上我们的生活和社会被混沌理论所左右，那么这个世界会更加充实。

<details>
<summary>View/Hide Original English</summary>
<p class="english-text">And so, I think the mentality we have towards the past is the correct one: that we are constantly reshaping the future, that what we do is important. And that idea is something that I think we are also somewhat allergic to because it's so overwhelming to imagine that every single act that we do has unforeseen ripple effects that will change the world and reshape our futures. It is bewildering, but it's also true. And one of the things that I think happens when you look closely at the nature of causality, reality, and existence is that things that are bewildering are happening all the time, and we just ignore them. And I think when you peer into them, you start to see a very different world, and one that can be more fulfilling if you actually accept that some things don't happen for a reason, and in fact, that we have a life and a society that is diverted by Chaos Theory.</p>
</details>

### 个人主义幻觉与相互关联的世界

西方现代性痴迷于我所称的**个人主义幻觉**（Delusion of Individualism: 认为个人完全独立且能完全掌控自身命运的错误信念），对许多人来说，认为自己掌控着自己的生活，是他们推动变革的主导者，而不是由他人决定你的生活如何展开，或者如果你是一名政治家，你可以控制经济的走向或选举的结果，这让他们感到安慰。

### 掌控幻觉：我们无法控制，但能影响一切

所以，这就是现实的本质，我认为这实际上是我们的一种应对机制，让我们忽略科学、忽略物理学，并想象我们掌控一切。而这正是我在《偶然》一书中反复提及的那句话的由来：我们无法控制任何事物，但我们能影响一切。对于伊万和那个足球来说，他所经历的影响来自于几个他从未谋面的孩子，他们挽救了他的生命。
