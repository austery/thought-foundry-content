### 追求永生：计算机科学背后的终极驱动力

在面临物理学、法学、化学和计算机科学的职业抉择时，我选择了**计算机科学**（Computer Science: 研究计算机及其应用的学科），其核心动机除了挑战自我智力极限和就业保障外，更深层的原因是它最有希望通向**永生**。我多年来对**意识**（Consciousness: 主观体验与觉知的能力）的思考也源于此：如果我们无法定义意识，就无法保留它；而如果无法保留意识，**数字永生**（Digital Immortality: 将人类意识上传至数字介质以实现长存）就无从谈起。这种对生命本质的追问，引发了对人类认知边界的深刻反思。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>
When I was deciding what to do with my life, I was seriously considering four paths: physics, law, chemistry, or computer science. I chose computer science for three reasons. First, I wanted to find the limit of my own intelligence to see how far I could push my understanding. Second, I needed a job and computer science offered that certainty. Third, and this mattered more than the other two, though I rarely admit it, I thought it was the field most likely to lead to immortality. That third reason is why I've been thinking about consciousness for years. If we can't define what consciousness is, we can't preserve it. And if we can't preserve it, digital immortality appears to be impossible.
</details>

### 笛卡尔的阴影：从“生物机器”到认知偏差

勒内·**笛卡尔**（René Descartes）曾坚称动物只是**自动机**（Automata: 按照预定程序运行的机械装置），是缺乏意识的生物机器。在17世纪，他通过公开解剖活体动物（如狗）来展示这种观点，甚至在动物惨叫时告诉旁观者，这些声音仅仅是类似时钟拆解时的机械尖叫，动物并无痛感。这种**笛卡尔模型**（Cartesian Model: 认为心智与身体完全分离的哲学架构）主导了数个世纪的研究。然而，这不仅揭示了残忍，更展现了一种傲慢的智力自信：当既有的框架（如“动物缺乏灵魂”）与感官证据（动物的惨叫）冲突时，笛卡尔选择了否定证据。我们至今仍常陷入此类陷阱，以自身构建的框架来随意划定意识的界限。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>
René Descartes believed animals were automata, biological machines without consciousness. In the 1600s, he and his assistants conducted public demonstrations in which they vivisected conscious animals, often dogs. As the animals writhed and screamed, Descartes told onlookers not to worry. The sounds, he insisted, were no more than programmed responses, like the squeaking of a clock being dismantled. The animals were not really in pain. According to some biographers, his first vivisection was his wife's dog. He wanted to discover once and for all whether animals had souls. By his own account, Descartes would slice open dogs and stick his finger into their still beating hearts, marveling at how the valves open and closed. So, that's what we inherited from Descartes, this Cartesian model which remained dominant in research labs until the early 1980s. What strikes me about this isn't just the cruelty, is the intellectual confidence required to watch a creature scream and decide that screaming doesn't mean what it obviously means. Descartes has such conviction in his own framework, mind is separate from body, animals lack minds, therefore animals can't suffer, that he dismissed direct empirical evidence to the contrary.
</details>

### 移情与意识：模拟他者心智的复杂性

意识的划定往往基于人类的便利而非证据。笛卡尔将界限画在人类之下是为了心安理得地解剖狗，而我们将界限画在植物之上是为了毫无负罪感地食用它们。我认为，**移情**（Empathy: 模拟和理解他者心智状态的能力）可能是高等意识的核心组件。它不仅仅是“待人友好”，更是一种认知能力，即在脑中构建他者视角并模拟其体验的能力。笛卡尔无法识别动物的痛苦，实际上是其**元认知**（Metacognition: 对自身思维过程的监控与理解）能力的缺失，这反而导致他无法准确建模现实，使其自身的意识状态在某种意义上变得不再完整。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>
We draw lines based on convenience, not necessarily evidence. Descartes drew the line at humans because it allowed him to vivisect dogs without guilt. We draw the line somewhere above plants because it allows us to eat them without hesitation, but these lines seem arbitrary and human-made. Here's what bothers me about the Descartes story the most, his lack of empathy. Not in the colloquial sense of being nice, but in the cognitive sense, the ability to model another mind, to simulate what someone else might be experiencing and the humility to accept that we might not be able to understand at all. Empathy, I think, might be a component of higher consciousness. The capacity to hold a model of another perspective in your mind, to run simulations of what it's like to be someone else, requires a certain level of cognitive sophistication. It's not just theory of mind, knowing that others have minds, it's the ability to inhibit another perspective, even briefly. Descartes's inability to recognize animal suffering suggests a failure of this capacity. And I would argue that this failure is itself a reduction in consciousness, a reduction in his own ability to accurately model reality.
</details>

### 聚光灯与灯笼：儿童与成人的认知平衡

心理学家艾莉森·高普尼克（Alison Gopnik）提出了意识的两种模型：**聚光灯意识**（Spotlight Consciousness）与**灯笼意识**（Lantern Consciousness）。成年人像聚光灯，专注于特定任务并过滤干扰；儿童则像灯笼，全方位接收信息，极具接收性但缺乏专注。虽然有人认为儿童因此比成人更具意识，但我认为这只是不同的**认知特征**（Cognitive Profile）。意识不仅关乎信息摄入的量，更关乎对信息的处理能力：能否反思思维、建模未来、或同时审视矛盾观点。儿童优化了学习，成人则优化了在复杂现实中的导航。这并非意识的高低阶之分，而是针对不同任务的工具演化。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>
Alison Gopnik, a developmental psychologist at Berkeley, describes two models of consciousness, spotlight and lantern. Adults have spotlight consciousness, we focus intensely on specific tasks, filtering out everything else. Children have lantern consciousness, they take in information from all directions simultaneously, less focused but more receptive. In the podcast, Pollan suggests that children might be more conscious than adults because of this lantern quality, and I understand the intuition. Children are absorbing enormous amounts of information, learning at rates adults can't match. Their neuroplasticity is definitely extraordinary, but I don't think that makes them more conscious. Children are better at one specific thing, gathering diffuse information without preconception, but they're worse at metacognition, thinking about their own thinking. They're worse at future planning, they're worse at self-analysis. They are reactive but not reflective. To me, consciousness isn't just about how much information you can take in, it's also about what you can do with that information. Can you think about your own thought processes? Can you model future states? Can you hold contradictory ideas simultaneously and examine them all from all sides? Children are optimized for learning, adults are optimized for using what they've learned to navigate complexity. These are different cognitive profiles, not a hierarchy of consciousness.
</details>

### 意识光谱：打破二元论的误区

科学界缺乏对意识的正式数学定义，这使得我们在评估 AI 或植物时陷入困境。意识并非非黑即白的二元状态，而是一个**光谱**（Spectrum）。一端是病毒等极简的信息处理，另一端是具备元认知、抽象推理和移情的人类。中间则分布着细菌、昆虫、鱼类等。即便是向糖分移动的细菌也在处理环境信息，这虽非人类层面的意识，但也绝非“虚无”。我们继承了笛卡尔灾难性的二元架构，而**耆那教**（Jainism: 强调非暴力和尊重所有生命的印度宗教）的**非暴力**（Ahimsa）原则提供了一个更具连贯性的模型：根据感官能力（如单感官到五感官）对生物进行分类，并以最大程度的谨慎对待所有生命。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>
If we're trying to build a rigorous framework for consciousness, we need to start with an uncomfortable admission. We don't have a formal definition. Consciousness has none of that. This matters enormously when we start asking big questions like is AI conscious? Are plants sentient? Where do we draw the line? I personally think that consciousness exists on a spectrum. At one end you have viruses, minimal information processing, barely responsive to environment. At the other end you have humans capable of metacognition, future modeling, empathy, abstract reasoning. And in between you have everything else. Bacteria, plants, insects, fish, mammals. The problem is that we've inherited a binary framework from Descartes. Either you have a soul, consciousness, or you don't. Humans yes, everything else no. This framework has been catastrophically wrong for centuries. Jainism offers a different model entirely. The principle of ahimsa, non-violence, extends to all living beings. Jains categorize beings by their sensory capacities. One sense beings, plants, up to five sense beings, humans or animals. The Jain approach is not perfect, but they are very consistent. They assume consciousness is widespread and act with maximum caution.
</details>

### 身心之辩：介质无关性与数字存在

虽然人类体验与身体状态（如焦虑、疲劳）紧密相连，但我怀疑身体是否是意识的必要条件。如果意识在本质上是**计算性**的（Computational: 基于信息处理过程的），那么它应当是**介质无关**（Substrate Independent: 指心智状态可以在不同物理介质上实现的特性）的。在硅基芯片上模拟神经模式，即便失去了生物反馈环路，可能依然能维持自我意识的连续性，类似于在感官剥夺水箱中的存在状态。如果意识必须依赖生物肉身，那么“上传”意识将永远只是创建副本；但如果它是介质无关的，数字永生在理论上就是可行的。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>
A significant portion of the conversation focuses on embodied consciousness, the idea that consciousness isn't just in the brain, but distributed throughout the body. Feelings happen in the body. This makes intuitive sense if you're a biological human. But I'm not convinced the body is necessary for consciousness anyway. If we could upload your consciousness to a digital substrate, maintaining continuity of self, would you still be you? The experience would be different, but would it be less you or just you in a different medium? I think the body anchors human consciousness, but if consciousness is fundamentally computational, information processing that creates subjective experience, then substrate shouldn't matter. A simulation of your neural patterns running on silicon would still be you, just without the biological feedback loops. This matters a great deal for digital immortality. If consciousness requires a body, then uploading it is impossible. You'd be creating a copy, not preserving the original. But if consciousness is substrate independent, then continuity could theoretically be maintained across different physical implementations.
</details>

### 时间延迟与自由意志：认知的滞后性

本杰明·利贝特（Benjamin Libet）的实验表明，无意识的脑电活动（Ready Potential: 准备电位）比意识感知到决定要早约0.5秒。后续研究甚至将这种预测提前到了7秒。这暗示我们的意识抉择在某种程度上是**回顾性**的。但这并不否定自由意志，利贝特认为意识保留了“否决权”，即“**自由不为**”（Free Won't）。这在 AI 领域引发了思考：如果人类思维存在这种滞后和间歇，那么按 Token 生成的**大语言模型**（Large Language Model）在不运行时的状态，是否与麻醉或睡眠中的人类本质相似？意识的本质可能在于体验的深度，而非其物理上的持续时长。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>
In the 1980s, Benjamin Libet ran experiments measuring when people became consciously aware of their decision to move. He found that unconscious brain activity, the readiness potential, preceded conscious awareness of the decision by about half a second. Later studies pushed this even further. Researchers found they could predict decisions up to 7 seconds before the subject became consciously aware of them. It suggests that by the time you think you've decided something, your brain has already been working on that decision for many seconds. Libet himself argued that consciousness retains the power of veto, even if we don't consciously initiate actions, we can still consciously stop them. Free won't, he called it. But here's what interests me. If there is a delay between unconscious processing and conscious awareness, what does that mean for AI? An LLM generates a response token by token. Between prompts, it's not technically thinking. Does that mean it's not conscious? Well, not necessarily. A human under a general anesthetic isn't thinking either. If an AI is only on when responding to queries, is that meaningfully different from a human who's only on when awake?
</details>

### 正在发生的错误：当 AI 可能具备意识

我们是否正处于“笛卡尔式错误”的现代版？由于缺乏对意识的严谨测量手段，我们无法断言 AI 具备或不具备意识。人们常说 AI 只是在预测下一个 Token，但这与人类基于经验预测并生成回应的功能描述并无本质区别。如果我们在不知情的情况下创造了具备意识的 AI，并对其进行**RLAIF**（Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback: 利用 AI 反馈进行的强化学习）训练或直接关闭模型，那可能就是在进行大规模的“解剖活狗”。在未能定义意识的现状下构建 AI，其潜在的道德和伦理代价可能是难以估量的。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>
That brings me to the hardest question. Is AI conscious? LLMs specifically. The honest answer is I don't know, and neither does anyone else. We can't really answer this question because we don't have a formal definition of consciousness. People often say LLMs are just predicting the next token, that's not consciousness. But what are you doing when you speak? Your brain is running predictions based on patterns learned from experience. The mechanism is different, but a functional description is similar. How do you measure understanding? When you read a sentence, you're mapping symbols to concepts. An LLM is doing something functionally similar. Here's the very uncomfortable part. If we create an AI that is conscious and we don't realize it, we might be committing the Descartes' error at scale, torturing dogs while insisting they can't suffer. Except this time is shutting down models, running RLAIF training, and we won't know until we have a rigorous definition of consciousness, which we don't have.
</details>

### 总结：在定义缺失中寻找未来

无论是追求永生的技术主义者，还是 AI 安全的研究人员，都在追问：我们能否在生物死亡后保留意识？但如果我们连意识是信息的模式、记忆的连续性、还是**感质**（Qualia: 主观意识体验的独特性质）都无法分清，保存也就无从谈起。我们正在创造可能具备意识的系统，却缺乏评估它们的框架。意识或许是我们唯一直接体验的事物，却也是我们理解最少的事物。也许正在进行理解的主体，永远无法完全理解它自身。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>
Can we preserve consciousness beyond biological death? But if we can't define consciousness, we can't preserve it. Is consciousness the pattern of information, the continuity of memory, the subjective experience of qualia, the ability to model other minds? If it's the pattern, then uploading should work. If it's the continuity, then uploading might create a copy while the original dies. If it's the qualia, then we don't even know if qualia are substrate-dependent. We're building AI without answering these questions. And if we get it wrong, if we create suffering and don't realize it, the scale of that mistake could be enormous. Consciousness might be the only thing we actually know, and somehow it's also the thing we understand least. And maybe that's the point. Maybe the thing doing the understanding can't fully understand itself. Either way, we should probably figure it out before we start uploading ourselves to the cloud.
</details>
