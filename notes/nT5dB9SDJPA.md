### 什么是建设性分歧

**Julia Minson**: 我经常告诉人们，**建设性的分歧**是指那种能让双方在结束后仍愿意再次交谈的分歧。对吧？所以，它并不是要达成一致，不是要建立共识，也不是要寻找某种谈判后的妥协。它是指我们能否产生分歧，但这种方式让你在未来某个时刻还想跟我说话，我也还想跟你说话。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Julia Minson**: What I always tell people is that a constructive disagreement is a disagreement that leads the two parties to want to talk to each other again. Right? So, it's not reaching agreement. It's not building consensus. It's not finding a negotiated compromise. It's can we disagree, but in a way that makes you want to talk to me and makes me want to talk to you at some point in the future.

</details>

So that appreciating the fact that other people have different circumstances than you do and different preferences than you do um I think is a really useful thing to do.

### 天真现实主义：为什么我们无法理解他人

**主持人**: 你的研究找出了为什么我们大多数人都不太擅长理解他人观点的因由。请简要概括一下你关于这方面的发现。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Host**: So your research has identified reasons why most of us are pretty bad at understanding other people's perspectives. So just outline what you found about that.

</details>

**Julia Minson**: 当然。我的很多工作都基于一种古老的社会心理学理论，叫做“**天真现实主义**”（naive realism）。天真现实主义背后的核心观点是：人们认为自己“懂行”。他们天真地相信自己的感知就是客观现实的反映。我眼中的世界就是它真实的样子。我是一个聪明、理性、善良的人，我基本上是跟得上形势的，对吧？

这说得通，因为否则你该如何在这个世界上生存呢？但天真现实主义的问题在于，当我们遇到分歧时，我们不得不问：如果我是一个聪明、理性、基本上懂行的人，那我们为什么会产生分歧呢？

而那个问题的显而易见的答案就是：对方“不懂”。既然他们不懂，我就得去“修复”他们。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Julia Minson**: Sure. So a lot of my work is grounded in uh an old social psychology theory called naive realism. The idea behind naive realism is that people think that they get it. They like naively believe that their perceptions are a reflection of an objective reality. Right? I see the world as it is. I am a smart, reasonable, good person who basically like I'm with the program, right? And that makes sense because how else could you get through the world? The problem with naive realism is that when we encounter disagreement, we have to say, well, if I'm a smart, reasonable person who basically gets it, why are we disagreeing? And the obvious answer to that question is that the other person doesn't get it.

</details>

**主持人**: 既然他们不懂，我就要纠正他们。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Host**: Right? And because they don't get it, I am going to fix them.

</details>

**Julia Minson**: 没错。当然，“修复”有不同的形式。有时你会说：“让我告诉你事实到底是怎么回事。让我给你看报告，让我给你看数据，让我给你看客户反馈。”然后你就会发现我是对的，你是错的。

但这通常行不通，因为对方当然也认为自己“懂”。他们也看过数据，只是以不同的方式解读了数据。他们有着多年的经验主导着他们对形势的理解，并且他们优先考虑的事情也不同。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Julia Minson**: Right? And of course, fixing then takes different forms. So sometimes you say, "Well, let me tell you how it really is. Let me show you the report. Let me show you the data. Let me give you the customer feedback." And then you will see that I'm right and you're wrong. Right? And that often doesn't fly because of course the other person, they also think that they get it. Uh and they've seen the data and they've interpreted the data in a different way, right? And they have sort of years of experience that dictate their understanding of the situation and they prioritize different things.

</details>

**主持人**: 优先级不同。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Host**: They prioritize different things.

</details>

**Julia Minson**: 没错。所以，当你发现单纯告诉人们事实不起作用时，你就会跳到另外几个更恶毒的结论。其中一个是：也许他们没那么聪明。他们上的是比较次的商学院，所以我们可以原谅他们不擅长理解复杂的关系。

或者更糟糕的是，他们有偏见。他们不想懂。如果他们承认我是对的，对他们来说面子上过不去。他们的预算会被削减，或者他们的员工会被缩减，或者他们不得不承认我更适合这个领导岗位。所以他们只是有意地不想“见到光明”。

一旦你在脑子里编造了这样的故事，就很难在成年人之间进行一场尊重的谈话了。而与此同时，我们也看不到他们是如何看待我们的——他们也带着同样的判断来看待我们。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Julia Minson**: Exactly. Exactly. And so, you know, when just sort of telling people facts doesn't work, uh, you jump to, you know, a couple other conclusions that are even more nefarious, which is, you know, one is, well, maybe they're not that smart. They went to the lesser business school and so we can forgive them for not being very good at understanding, you know, complex relationships or even worse, they are biased. They don't want to get it. You know, if they admit that I am right, it will look bad for them. You know, their budget will get cut or their staff will get cut or they will have to recognize that I'm a better fit for this leadership position. Uh and so they are just sort of intentionally not seeing the light. Yeah. Right. And so once you tell that story to yourself inside your head, it's very hard to have a respectful conversation between adults. Yeah. And at the same time, we also don't see how they are perceiving us, which is with all those same judgments. Right.

</details>

### 关注行为：沟通的“投资回报率”

**主持人**: 你书中的一个主要观点是，为了更好地产生分歧，你需要关注的不是你的心态或情绪，而是你的**行为**。我们最近采访了圣三一大学的 Amar Kisy，他谈到了积极意图心态的重要性。为什么你认为聚焦于行为比聚焦于提前思考和感受更重要？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Host**: Exactly. Exactly. Exactly. That's exactly right. So, one of the main points of your book and your article is that to disagree better, you need to focus on not your mindset or your emotions, but on your behaviors. We recently did a podcast with Amar Kisy of Trinity University who talked about the importance of positive intent mindset. Why do you think that narrowing down on the behaviors rather than sort of what you're thinking and feeling ahead of time is most important?

</details>

**Julia Minson**: 因为老实说，以一种非常务实的方式来说，我关注的是 **ROI**（投资回报率）。

我认为如果我们都有积极的意图，对对手有更温暖的评价，并有更好的情绪自我控制，世界会变得更美好。只是那是非常、非常艰苦的工作。而且它需要很长时间，并且有一件事会阻碍它，那就是**人际感知**。

即使我对你抱有最友善、最美好、最有诚意的想法，仍然需要发生两件事才能影响我们的互动。一是我必须能够表达出所有这些。我必须一贯地表达，并带有你能理解的信号。然后你必须解读这个信号。在这个链条的每一步，我们都会丢失信息并误解对方。

常见的情况是，我为一场预期会很困难的对话做准备，对自己说：“我要非常有耐心，我要保持好奇，我要展现智识上的谦逊，我要表现同理心。”然后我走进谈话，对方一开口，我的一半意图就抛到九霄云外了。

因为我仍然在努力，但我又不是得道高僧，我对情绪没有惊人的控制力，我只有中等水平的控制力。然后我试着表现出同理心和好奇心，但我可能会不小心问出一个刻薄的问题。我本意不是要刻薄，但听起来就是很刻薄。而你正在读解这一切，当然，这是一场分歧。所以，你对我抱有负面的期望。所以我问的那个有点刻薄的问题，在你的感知里就变得超级刻薄。
