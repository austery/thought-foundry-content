### 大脑的本色：为何我们会做梦？

**David Eagleman**: 经过几十年的争论，人们可能终于弄清楚了我们**为什么会做梦**。答案其实很简单。如果你失明了，大脑后部的**视觉皮层**会被听觉、触觉和其他感官接管。事实上，我们在哈佛的同事做过一个实验，他们把视力正常的人蒙上眼睛，仅 60 分钟后，你就能看到这种“接管”开始发生。那时我们意识到，做梦的目的是为了**捍卫视觉领地**，防止被其他感官侵占。但我对大脑可塑性最着迷的地方，也是我整个职业生涯致力于研究的，是寻找让我们成为自己大脑“雕刻师”的方法，这给了我们成为理想中自己的机会。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: After many many decades of people debating this, you might have figured out the reason why we dream. Yes. And it's a simple answer. So if you go blind, the visual cortex in the back of the brain gets taken over by hearing and by touch and by other things. In fact, our colleagues at Harvard did an experiment where they blindfolded normally cighted people. And you could start seeing that takeover happening after 60 minutes. And that's when we realized, wow, the purpose of dreaming is to defend the visual territory from takeover from the other senses. But what fascinates me about brain plasticity and what I've devoted my career to is figuring out the way that we can be the sculptors of our own brains and how it gives us an opportunity to become the kind of person we would like to be.

</details>

**Stephen Bartlett**: 我们真的能做到吗？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: And can we do that?

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 是的。关键点在于：你的大脑在两岁时达到巅峰。起初你拥有**流体智力**（Fluid Intelligence），意味着你可以学习任何东西。但现在你在这个世界上长大，拥有了**晶体智力**（Crystallized Intelligence），意味着你知道如何开车、操作手机、经营业务。因此，你的大脑不再需要那么多改变，这意味着大脑结构总是在退化。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: Yes. Here's the thing. Your brain peaked at the age of two. Okay. So at the beginning you've got fluid intelligence, meaning you could learn anything. But now that you have grown up in this world, you've got crystallized intelligence, meaning you know how to drive a car. You know how to operate a cell phone. You know how to run a business. And so your brain doesn't require as much change which means that the structure of the brain is always degenerating.

</details>

### 团队竞争：理解内心的矛盾

**Stephen Bartlett**: 那么，有哪些行为可以从根本上改变我的大脑，让我成为那种充满动力、自律、具有高度主动性并积极应对世界的人？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: So what are the set of actions that will fundamentally change my brain and make me that type of person who's motivated and disciplines and who has high agency and attacks the world.

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 这是我在实验室研究了数十年的课题。关键在于，我们要意识到自己并非不可分割的个体，而是一个**“竞争团队”**（Team of Rivals）。你的大脑中有 860 亿个神经元，它们以极快的速度交流。问题是，你会产生相互竞争的网络。比如，如果我把巧克力曲奇放在你面前，你大脑的一部分想吃它（良好的能量来源），另一部分说“别吃，会变胖”，还有一部分说“吃一个，晚上去健身房”。重点是，你在和自己争论。这种冲突正是人类有趣的原因——我们有这么多声音在试图驱动不同的行为。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: So this is something I've studied in my lab for decades now. And the key is that... One of the things that I started writing about years ago is that I think we often think of ourselves as individuals, meaning not divisible into other things. But really, you are a team of rivals. So, you've got all these neural networks that have different drives making different suggestions to you... In the brain, you've got 86 billion cells called neurons... you can actually get competing networks. So, for example, Stephen, if I drop some chocolate chip cookies in front of you, part of your brain wants to eat it. It's a good energy source. Part of your brain says, "Don't eat it. I'll gain weight." Part of you says, "Okay, I'll eat one, but I'll go to the gym tonight." The point is you are arguing with yourself. You are conflicted. This is what makes humans so interesting is that we have all these voices trying to drive us to different conclusions about our behavior.

</details>

### 尤利西斯契约：约束未来的自己

**David Eagleman**: 理解这一点对导航我们的人生至关重要。这引出了所谓的**“尤利西斯契约”**（Ulisses Contract）。它是指你现在采取行动，以防止自己在不久的将来表现不佳。例如，匿名戒酒协会告诉人们的第一件事就是：清理掉家里所有的酒。即便你现在处于清醒的反思时刻，觉得永远不想再喝，但在某个寂寞的周日晚上，你可能会失控。所以，你通过设置正确的环境来约束未来的行为。拥有这种“审视引擎盖下发生什么”的意识，能让我们更接近理想中的自己。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: The way that your ship of state moves depends on the vote of the neural parliament at any time. So understanding this I think is really critical to navigating our own lives... Part of what this leads to is what we call the Ulisses contract. So a Ulisses contract is where you do something now to prevent yourself from behaving badly in the near future. Just as an example, you know, when people go to Alcoholics Anonymous, the first thing they're told is clear all the alcohol out of the house... So, what you do is you constrain your future behavior by setting things up in the right way so your future uh the future you can't behave badly... having a sense of what's going on under the hood gives us an opportunity to be more closely aligned with the kind of person we would like to be.

</details>

### 大脑可塑性：为什么成年后改变很难？

**Stephen Bartlett**: 关于大脑，人们一直以来有哪些重大的误解？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: What are the big misconceptions about the brain that people have gone through their life believing?

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 一个核心概念是**“脑可塑性”**（Brain Plasticity）。心理学家 **William James** 在 100 年前指出，大脑就像塑料一样，可以被塑造成某种形状并保持住。我们的一生，大脑每时每刻都在改变。但有些“门”会在不同时期关闭，比如语言学习。如果你在生命的最初几年没有接触语言，你的大脑可能永远无法掌握语言的概念。

人类大脑的特殊之处在于我们拥有更多的**皮层**（Cortex）和极强的适应能力。马出生时，它做的和十万年前的马没区别；但人类出生时，我们会学习前人所有的成果，并以此为跳板。代价是人类大脑在出生时是“半熟”的，我们必须吸收环境信息。大脑的任务是建立世界模型以进行操作。随着年龄增长，大脑改变变少不是因为失去了可塑性，而是因为它已经找到了在这个世界上运行的“正确答案”，**不需要**再改变那么多。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: Brain plasticity... the reason it came about 100 years ago is because the great psychologist William James pointed out that, you know, if you take a piece of plastic... you can mold it into a shape and it'll hold that shape. And that's what your brain does... our whole lives our brains are changing every moment. So now we have certain doors that close at different times. So just as an example, um you need to learn language in the first several years of your life. If you don't learn language, you can never get the concept of language... Human beings have a similar brain to all our neighbors in the animal kingdom... but what we have is much more of the wrinkly outer bit called the cortex... we have much more plasticity, adaptability such that when a horse drops into the world, it's doing the same thing that horses did 100,000 years ago. But when a human drops in the world, we learn everything that's happened before us. And then we springboard off the top of that... The reason brains change less and less is because they don't have to. But when things get upside down... everything about the pandemic really stunk, except for one thing... all of us had to reassess... challenging your internal model of the world and get to do that as an adult.

</details>

### 如何重塑大脑：寻求“有益的摩擦”

**Stephen Bartlett**: 如果我想改变，比如我是一个固执、缺乏动力的人，你想让我做什么？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: So, if I want to change, what would you recommend that I do? If I want to change who I am, say I'm stubborn, I'm not motivated...

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 关键是**挑战**和**寻求新奇**。你要让自己处于“令人沮丧但可实现”的水平之间，承担新任务。一旦你擅长某件事，你就必须放下它，去挑战你不擅长的事。这对大脑最好，因为它在不断建立新的通路。著名的“修女研究”发现，一些修女虽然在物理层面上患有阿尔茨海默病，但并没有表现出认知缺陷。因为她们直到去世都生活在充满社交挑战、游戏和责任的环境中，大脑一直在修路架桥。我们称之为**认知储备**（Cognitive Reserve）。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: The key is challenge. The key is seeking challenge. So, it turns out that where we always want to be is in between the levels of frustrating but achievable. And you want to take on new tasks. You want to seek novelty... the key is that once you become good at something, you you have to drop that and take on something you're not good at. This is the best thing that you can do for your brain... There's a study... called the religious orders study where a bunch of Catholic nuns agreed to donate their brains for autopsy... some fraction of these nuns had Alzheimer's disease... but they didn't show any of the cognitive deficits... because all these nuns lived in these convents till the day they died. They had social challenges... and they were doing stuff... we call that cognitive reserve.

</details>

### AI 时代的大脑：摩托车还是替代品？

**Stephen Bartlett**: 我在想，如果我在商业生活中遇到困难，每次都把它丢给 AI，让它给出答案。我是不是跳过了重要的学习周期或神经发育过程？我是在逃避你说的“困难的事”。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: But one of the things I've wondered is if I'm going through my business life and I'm encountering hard problems and every time I encounter a hard problem, I drop it into an AI... presumably there's some kind of important part of the learning cycle... that I'm like foregoing there... I'm avoiding the hard thing which is like thinking about it and trying to understand it.

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 这里有一个非常重要的区分：**恶意摩擦**（Vicious Friction）与**良性摩擦**（Virtuous Friction）。恶意摩擦是那些无聊的琐事，比如整理表格、算税。把这些交给 AI 对人类生活至关重要。但良性摩擦是思考业务的最优方式，这是你在动脑学习。AI 应该是一个全天候待命的专家，和你一起探讨问题，而不是让你复制粘贴。

如果说个人电脑是大脑的“自行车”，那么 AI 就是大脑的**“摩托车”**。它让我们跑得更快。现在是一场摩托车赛，那些只会复制粘贴“AI 垃圾”的人很容易被击败。我们要利用 AI 挑战自己的盲点，要求它“对我保持残酷的诚实，告诉我为什么我可能是错的”。这才是真正的成长。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: Yeah here's I think the really important distinction: there's vicious friction in our lives and there's virtuous friction. So vicious friction is all the stupid stuff that you have to do... if we can push that off to AI is massively important for improving human lives. But virtuous friction is... "Stephen, I really want you to think about what is the optimal way to do this business." ...that's virtuous friction because you're really using your brain to learn stuff that way... What Steve Jobs introduced Apple computers, he said this is like a bicycle for the mind... and I think of AI now as like a motorcycle for the mind. It allows us to move so much faster. So now it's a motorcycle race... well, for sure the people who are just copying and pasting the AI slop that'll be easy to beat that crowd... the prompt that always I think is most sort of expansive... is when I say to it, be brutally honest about your opinion... tell me where my blind spots are.

</details>

### 人性的回归：IRL 的复兴

**Stephen Bartlett**: 也许 AI 带来的有趣悖论是：以往的所有技术迭代都让我们变得更不具人性，而现在的技术已经好到迫使我们**回归人性**，因为那是唯一剩下的东西。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: I did a post on LinkedIn the other day saying that maybe the like interesting paradox or interesting outcome of AI is that every other iteration of technology made us less human. And maybe the intelligence now has gotten to a point where... it's now forcing us to be more human because that is all that kind of remains...

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 我完全同意。我预测未来会有**现场剧院和现场表演的复兴**。人们想看到真实的 Taylor Swift。虽然你可以用 AI 制作自己的化身去做演讲，但没人想要那个。人们想让演讲者飞过半个国家，亲自站在那，因为见到同类非常重要。AI 会接管管理和文书工作，让护士和医生去握住病人的手，提供只有人类能给出的关怀。我们拥有数百万年的进化驱动力，让我们渴望与所爱之人在一起、仰望星空、与父母共进晚餐。这种对真实连接的渴望是不会改变的。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: I totally agree. And by the way... there's going to be actually a renaissance in things like live theater and live performances... why? Because they're going to see the real Taylor Swift in person... more people want to fly us across the country to have us stand there in person because it really matters to see fellow humans. And I think that's only going to increase... maybe they shouldn't be filling out admin and paperwork anymore. Maybe they should be holding your hand and giving you, you know, in real life care that only a human could do.

</details>
