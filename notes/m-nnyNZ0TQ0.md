### 大脑的秘密与梦的防御机制

**David Eagleman**: 经过几十年的争论，人们可能终于弄清楚了我们**为什么会做梦**。答案其实很简单。如果你失明了，大脑后部的**视觉皮层**会被听觉、触觉等其他感官占领。事实上，哈佛大学的同事做过一个实验，他们把视力正常的人眼睛蒙上，仅仅 **60 分钟**后，你就能看到这种占领开始发生。那时我们意识到，做梦的目的是为了**保卫视觉领地**，防止被其他感官吞噬。我对**大脑可塑性**非常着迷，我职业生涯的大部分时间都在研究如何让我们成为自己大脑的**雕刻家**，如何通过它成为我们想成为的人。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: After many many decades of people debating this, you might have figured out the reason why we dream. Yes. And it's a simple answer. So if you go blind, the visual cortex in the back of the brain gets taken over by hearing and by touch and by other things. In fact, our colleagues at Harvard did an experiment where they blindfolded normally cighted people. And you could start seeing that takeover happening after 60 minutes. And that's when we realized, wow, the purpose of dreaming is to defend the visual territory from takeover from the other senses. But what fascinates me about brain plasticity and what I've devoted my career to is figuring out the way that we can be the sculptors of our own brains and how it gives us an opportunity to become the kind of person we would like to be.

</details>

**Stephen Bartlett**: 我们真的能做到吗？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: And can we do that?

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 是的。关键点在于：你的大脑在**两岁**时达到巅峰。起初你拥有**流体智能**，意味着你可以学习任何东西。但现在你在这个世界上长大，你拥有了**晶体智能**，你知道如何开车、操作手机、经营业务。因此你的大脑不再需要那么多改变，这意味着大脑结构总是处于**退化**中。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: Yes. Here's the thing. Your brain peaked at the age of two. Okay. So at the beginning you've got fluid intelligence, meaning you could learn anything. But now that you have grown up in this world, you've got crystallized intelligence, meaning you know how to drive a car. You know how to operate a cell phone. You know how to run a business. And so your brain doesn't require as much change which means that the structure of the brain is always degenerating.

</details>

### 大脑：相互竞争的团队

**Stephen Bartlett**: 那么，有哪些行为能从根本上改变我的大脑，让我成为那种有动力、有纪律、有**高执行力**并勇于挑战世界的人？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: So what are the set of actions that will fundamentally change my brain and make me that type of person who's motivated and disciplines and who has high agency and attacks the world.

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 这是一个我在实验室研究了几十年的课题。关键在于，我们通常认为自己是不可分割的个体，但实际上，你是**一个相互竞争的团队**。你拥有许多具有不同驱动力的神经网络，它们在向你提出不同的建议。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: So this is something I've studied in my lab for decades now. And the key is that... yeah, One of the things that I started writing about years ago is that I think we're not... I think we often think of ourselves as individuals, meaning not divisible into other things. But really, you are a team of rivals. So, you've got all these neural networks that have different drives making different suggestions to you.

</details>

**Stephen Bartlett**: 什么是神经网络？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: What's a neural network?

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 大脑中有 **860 亿个神经元**，它们以极快的速度交流。许多细胞连接成网络。问题在于，你会产生相互竞争的网络。比如 Stephen，如果我把巧克力曲奇放在你面前，你大脑的一部分想吃它，因为它是优质能量；另一部分说“别吃，会变胖”；还有一部分说“吃一个，晚上去健身房”。重点是你在**自我争论**。这就是人类有趣的地方——我们有所有这些声音试图驱使我们对行为得出不同的结论。

你的“国家之船”如何航行，取决于当时**神经议会**的投票结果。理解这一点对导航人生至关重要，因为我们都会做一些事后后悔的事，那是因为不同时期由不同的声音掌权。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: Um, so in the brain, you've got 86 billion cells called neurons. And these are communicating with each other at a blindingly fast rate. Many of these cells are hooked up in networks. So, they're, you know, this guy's talking to this guy and this guy, and they're all in particular networks. The thing is, you can actually get competing networks. So, for example, Stephen, if I drop some chocolate chip cookies in front of you, part of your brain wants to eat it. It's a good energy source. Part of your brain says, "Don't eat it. I'll gain weight." Part of you says, "Okay, I'll eat one, but I'll go to the gym tonight." The point is you are arguing with yourself. You are conflicted. This is what makes humans so interesting is that we have all these voices trying to drive us to different conclusions about our behavior. The way that your ship of state moves depends on the vote of the neural parliament at any time. So understanding this I think is really critical to navigating our own lives because all of us do things where retrospectively we regret it.

</details>

### 尤利西斯契约与自我约束

**David Eagleman**: 这种理解引出了所谓的**尤利西斯契约 (Ulysses Contract)**。它是指你现在采取行动，以防止自己在不久的将来表现不佳。例如，匿名戒酒协会要求的第一件事就是清理家中所有的酒。因为即使你现在处于清醒的自我反省中，如果你家里有酒，在某个寂寞的周日晚上，你最终会打开酒柜。所以，你通过正确地设置环境来**约束未来的行为**。我们天真地认为自己只是一个人，但事实并非如此。在不同环境下，你会受到不同事物的诱惑。了解大脑“引擎盖”下的运作，给了我们一个机会，让我们更接近想成为的那种人。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: Part of what this leads to is what we call the Ulisses contract. So a Ulisses contract is where you do something now to prevent yourself from behaving badly in the near future. Just as an example, you know, when people go to Alcoholics Anonymous, the first thing they're told is clear all the alcohol out of the house. Because even if you feel like, look, I'm in a moment of sober reflection. I don't want to ever drink again. If you have alcohol in the house, you're going to bust into that cabinet at some point on a festive Saturday night or a lonely Sunday night or whatever. So, what you do is you constrain your future behavior by setting things up in the right way so your future... the future you can't behave badly. We naively think, okay, well, I know who I am. I'm just one person. But but you're not. And under different circumstances, you're tempted by different things and you'll do different kinds of behavior. So having a sense of what's going on under the hood gives us an opportunity to be more closely aligned with the kind of person we would like to be.

</details>

### 挑战与认知储备

**Stephen Bartlett**: 如果我想改变，你会推荐我做什么？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: So, if I want to change, what would you recommend that I do?

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 关键在于**挑战**，在于主动寻求挑战。理想的状态是处于“令人沮丧但可以实现”的水平之间。你需要承担新任务，寻求新鲜感。一旦你擅长某件事，你就必须放弃它，去挑战你不擅长的事。这对大脑最好，因为你正在不断建立新的**路网和桥梁**。

有一项针对天主教修女的研究显示，有些修女死后解剖发现大脑患有**阿尔茨海默症**，物理上已经退化，但在生前她们没有任何认知缺陷。这是因为她们一直生活在修道院里，有社交挑战、责任、集体游戏和争吵。这意味着即使大脑组织在退化，她们也在不断修筑新路。我们称之为**认知储备 (Cognitive Reserve)**。相比之下，那些 65 岁退休回家看电视、社交圈萎缩的人，因为没有建立新路径，情况往往令人担忧。**社交生活**是大脑能做的最重要的事之一，因为没有什么比“其他人”更让大脑感到困难了，你永远不知道对方会说什么或做什么。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: The key is challenge. The key is seeking challenge. So, it turns out that where we always want to be is in between the levels of frustrating but achievable. and you want to take on new tasks. You want to seek novelty to find yourself in that zone and push yourself to do things that you just haven't done before. And and I think the key is that once you become good at something, you you have to drop that and take on something you're not good at. This is the best thing that you can do for your brain. The reason is because what you're doing is you're constantly building new roadways and pathways in the brain... What that means is even as the tissue the brain tissue was physically degenerating, they were making new roadways and bridges all the time. And so that's what kept them cognitively healthy. We call that cognitive reserve. Contrast this with with people who retire at 65 and they go home and they watch television and their social circles shrink and so on. That's when you've really got concerns because you're not building the new pathways.

</details>

### AI：思维的“摩托车”

**Stephen Bartlett**: 关于 AI 和社交媒体对大脑发育的影响，你怎么看？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: And what about AI and the social media debate as it relates to brain development?

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 我对年轻人持**网络乐观主义**态度。我认为这会让他们比前几代人更聪明。原因在于他们摄取的**智力食物**规模。我小时候要查资料得去图书馆翻大百科全书，那是“以防万一”的知识；现在的孩子只要好奇，随时随地都能通过语音助手得到答案。当你有好奇心时，大脑会产生正确的化学反应，知识更容易固化。

关于 AI 会让我们变懒的担忧，我的观点是区分**恶性摩擦 (Vicious Friction)** 和**良性摩擦 (Virtuous Friction)**。恶性摩擦是填表、算税等琐事，推给 AI 是对人类生活的巨大改进；良性摩擦是思考最优商业结构、解决从未遇到的难题，这需要大脑参与。AI 不是让你复制粘贴，而是作为**全人类知识的专家导师**与你协作。如果说个人电脑是思维的自行车，那么 AI 就是思维的**摩托车**，它让我们跑得更快。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: Well, I happen to be a cyber optimist for young people. I think it's going to make them much smarter than the generation that came before. And here's why. It has to do with the size of the intellectual diet that they can bring in... kids are in a really great situation now. So, there are pros and cons to all this stuff, but I think I'm very optimistic about what this means for the warehouse of knowledge that kids can build up now. I think of AI now as like a motorcycle for the mind... it allows us to move so much faster. So now it's a motorcycle race and there will be people who are much faster than other people because they're really using that optimally. There's vicious friction in our lives and there's virtuous friction. Vicious friction is all the stupid stuff... push that off to AI is massively important. But virtuous friction is thinking about the optimal way to do business... that's where you're really using your brain.

</details>

### 面对 AI 浪潮的人类优势

**Stephen Bartlett**: 既然每个人口袋里都有一个“亚里士多德”（AI），我们如何创造优势？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: If everyone's got Aristotle in their pocket, how does one create an edge?

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 那些只会复制粘贴 AI 垃圾内容的人很容易被打败。真正的优势在于**批判性思维和创造力**。创造力其实就是吸收世界的一切，然后对认知概念进行重新组合（Remix）。AI 具有极强的再创作能力，但它目前最缺乏的是**选择 (Selection)**——它不知道哪幅画最能触动你，哪首歌会成为下一年的流行趋势。

未来的悖论在于：技术越先进，反而越迫使我们**回归人性**。当 AI 处理了所有行政琐事，护士可以花更多时间握住病人的手给予关怀；当数字内容泛滥，人们会更渴望现场剧院、现场音乐会，去见真实的 Taylor Swift。**人与人的连接**是无法被算法替代的。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: I think it has to do with... we're all just going to be running faster. Well, for sure the people who are just copying and pasting the AI slop that'll be easy to beat that crowd. What we can teach is critical thinking and creativity. That's it. Creativity in the brain, all creativity is is you absorb your world... and then you're bending and breaking and blending those cognitive concepts into new remixes. The part of creativity that AI can't do right now is selection. Meaning it can generate a 100 pictures but it doesn't know which one to pick... maybe the interesting paradox of AI is that every other iteration of technology made us less human. And maybe the intelligence now has gotten to a point where it's now forcing us to be more human because that is all that kind of remains.

</details>

### 个体差异：幻念症与联觉

**David Eagleman**: 人类社会的狂野之处在于我们之间共同点很少，每个人都非常不同。我研究这种微妙的差异几十年了。比如**幻念症 (Aphantasia)**，如果你闭上眼想象一只蚂蚁，我大脑里没有任何视觉图像。我处于光谱的一端。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: The wild part about human society is that there's so little that we have in common, meaning everybody is really different. I'll just give you an example like if I ask you to visualize let's say an ant... I happen to be at the other end of that spectrum called aphantasia where I don't have any visual images at all.

</details>

**Stephen Bartlett**: 我能看到一只巨大的黑蚂蚁正爬向溢出果酱的罐子，连木头盖子都清晰可见。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: I see a big black ant and then this jar of jelly is like overflowing down the sides with a wooden lid on top of it and the ant is almost there.

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 那你处于**超幻念症 (Hyperphantasia)** 一端。有趣的是，这并不影响你的能力。Pixar 创始人 Ed Catmull 也是幻念症患者。幻念症的孩子可能会因为必须观察实物而更擅长绘画。此外还有 **3%** 的人拥有**联觉 (Synesthesia)**，感官会发生融合，比如看到字母会触发颜色体验。这些都不是疾病，只是另一种感知现实的方式。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: Oh wow. So you have a... you are on the end of the spectrum that we call hyperfantasia which means you have very rich visualization. It turns out that if you are an aphantasia kid, you're going to become better at drawing because you have to really pay attention to the subject... Sesthesia is having a blending of the senses. At least 3% of the population has this. It's not a disease or a disorder. It's just an alternative perceptual reality.

</details>

### 总结与愿景

**Stephen Bartlett**: 对于想要改善生活的人，你有什么最后的建议？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**Stephen Bartlett**: Dr. David, what is the most important thing we haven't talked about... specifically relates to people that are trying to improve their lives.

</details>

**David Eagleman**: 保持大脑活跃，直到去世的那一天。不断接受新挑战，一旦擅长了某件事（如数独），就放弃它并开始你不擅长的事。强迫大脑做出改变。我希望未来 10 年，我们能通过 AI 挑战自己的偏见，复杂化我们与“外群体”的关系。理解对方不代表同意，但我们可以学着把对方看作一个鲜活的人，而不是一个需要绕过的物体。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**David Eagleman**: Keep your brain active. Keep it active till the day you die. Take on new challenges. And as soon as you get good at something like, you know, sudoku, drop it and pick up some that you're not good at. You're forcing your brain to make changes. I think I would come back to this piece about the complexification of relationships... if we can just get to that point, at least we'd have slightly less polarization. And I do think AI can help us get there by challenging us on these points.

</details>
