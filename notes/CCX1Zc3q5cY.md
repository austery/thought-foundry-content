### 速度与直觉：从发布到百万美金 ARR 的 117 天

**Yasser Elsaid** 分享了 **Chatbase** 极具爆发力的开局。在不到三年的时间里，他在下午 1 点正式发布产品，仅 30 分钟后就获得了第一位付费客户。当一小时内接连出现第三个客户时，他意识到这并非偶然，必须放下生活中其他的一切，全力以赴抓住这个特殊的机会。在完全没有外部融资（Bootstrapped）的情况下，Chatbase 仅用 **117 天** 就实现了 100 万美元的 **年度经常性营收**（ARR: Annual Recurring Revenue）。

他认为，当前正处于一个技术爆发点，AI 工具极大地提升了个人和小型团队的生产效率。未来我们将看到大量由 10 到 50 人组成的“小而美”公司，凭借极高的人均产出（Revenue per employee），创造出此前只有大型企业才能实现的营收规模。这种模式不仅让创始人拥有绝对的**控制权**，更能让他们专注于为客户创造价值，而非受制于风险投资（VC）对成功定义的改变。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

We started now close to 3 years ago. I launched I think at like 1:00 p.m. I got my first customer 30 minutes after that. I think 10 minutes after I got the second person, maybe an hour after I got the third person. At this moment, I knew that I need to stop everything else I'm doing in life. We did 1 million ARR in exactly 117 days. We've been growing ever since bootstrapped without any external funding. It's just me and the team. That's it. My opinion is that you'll see an explosion of more and more bootstrapped companies doing insane numbers of revenue that were not possible before.

Chatbase is a platform for doing customer-facing AI agents specifically for customer support and sales. So, we're building an AI agent that is a brand ambassador and gives that conversational experience to every customer. We just bootstrapped Chatbase to $10 million in ARR.

The biggest advantage [of bootstrapping] is that you have control. So you only listen to your customers and the team and that's it. And I think this gives you a lot of freedom in building exactly what you want to build. And I think the other reason is the definition of success changes as soon as you raise. When you're bootstrapping like a successful outcome is like 50 millionaire or 100 millionaire or even 200 which I think is very possible for us. But I think raising changes that equation a lot.

### 独立开发者的陷阱：警惕过度节俭的“Bootstrapper 心态”

虽然独立开发强调盈利和效率，但 Yasser 认为最大的错误往往在于**过于谨小慎微**。初期关注投资回报率（ROI）和银行余额是正确的，但一旦有了稳定的现金流，创始人必须迅速摆脱这种“Bootstrapper 心态”。

如果你的目标是建立具有巨大影响力的事业，那么**不够激进**就是最大的失误。一旦业务步入正轨，就必须敢于进行可能无法立即盈利的实验，聘用昂贵但顶尖的人才。Yasser 强调，Chatbase 曾决定在旧金山购买高昂的路牌广告以提升品牌意识，虽然这在短期内会显著降低利润率，但从长期来看，这是构建大公司必经的**风险投资**。这种从“成本驱动”向“增长驱动”的转变，是 Chatbase 突破 1000 万美元营收的关键。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

When you operate as a bootstrap company, you're more focusing on profitability. everything you do is ROI positive. You're making sure you have money in the bank. You're making sure to be as efficient as possible. The most common mistake bootstrap founders make is having the mindset of a bootstrap founder. That means being extremely cost efficient with everything. Always trying to make sure you're ROI positive, being risk averse.

But if you want to build something huge and you want to have as much impact, hire people, see revenue go up, then the biggest mistake is not being aggressive enough. It makes sense to have that mindset early on. But I think you should very quickly stop having that as soon as you have some revenue you can rely on. You have to take risks. You have to do experiments that might not be ROI positive. You have to hire people that you think are expensive because they're good. This is what we stopped doing and we've seen a lot of revenue growth since we started acting that way.

</details>

### 从大厂实习到独立开发者：构建真实的创造力

在加拿大攻读计算机科学期间，Yasser 曾遵循“大厂实习”的标准路径，先后在 **Tesla** 和 **Facebook** 完成实习。尽管这些经历在外人看来光鲜亮丽，但他很快发现这种高度结构化的环境并不适合自己。大厂的职级系统（如从 E3 工程师到 E6 工程师）虽然伴随着高薪和地位，但生活轨迹过于可预测，本质上是在优化获得 20 万美元年薪的概率。

受 **Peter Levels** 等独立黑客（Indie Hackers）的启发，他意识到自己更渴求**创造的能力**。他认为，建立公司的技能只能通过“建立公司”本身来学习，在大公司习得的技能往往是无关紧要的。他极度推崇“输入与产出高度相关”的模式，这种动态感与竞技体育高度相似：你赢是因为你训练得好，你输是因为你做得不够。这种掌控自己命运的信念，是他从“打工人”转型为“创造者”的核心动力。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

I started university in Canada studying computer science. Everyone basically had the same goal: getting a FANG internship in California. I did an internship at Tesla. I did another one at Facebook. But I think very quickly I did realize that maybe this is not the environment for me. It just felt too safe, too structured. It's mostly optimizing for the highest likelihood of making like above $200,000 a year.

I just wanted this ability to create things. I saw this community of indie hackers, especially on Twitter. People like Peter Levels or Mark Louw, they have an idea and they sit down and build it and then they share it with the world. You learn how to build a company by building a company. I think any skill that you learn at a big company is mostly irrelevant because it's just like a completely different dynamic. In building a company, especially when you're solo, the outcome is like 100% correlated by the input you put in. Knowing that you control your destiny gives you comfort.

</details>

### Chatbase 的起源：在 AI 浪潮爆发前捕捉机会

早在 **ChatGPT** 成名之前的 2022 年，Yasser 就敏锐地发现了 **GPT-3** 等通用大模型的局限性：它们拥有强大的通识，却缺乏针对特定公司、个人或文档的私有数据。Chatbase 的最初版本非常直观——让用户上传一本教科书，然后与其直接对话。

他当时就确立了一个核心策略：**面向未来的模型进行开发**（Building for the next model）。他意识到，虽然当时的模型能力有限，但他构建的“应用外壳”（Harness）会随着底层模型能力的提升而自动增值。这种前瞻性的思考让他比大多数人更早进入赛道。他甚至为此退出了大学课堂，将全部精力投入到产品的打磨中。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

When I was building these other projects, some of them were using AI before ChatGPT was launched. It became obvious to me that there is this one idea: you have these powerful language models that are trained on all this general data, but they don't have specific data to a specific person or company. The very first version of Chatbase was uploading a textbook or a book and then just chatting with it.

Now everyone knows if you're building an AI you're building for the next model and the next model so that every time the model improves your product improves. To me, it was obvious that these models will improve. I didn't know how, I didn't know when, but if this model improves and I built the harness around it that makes it able to do these cool new things, then I'm winning.

</details>

### 增长战术：有机营销与“暖意”主动开发

在最初的三个月里，由于没有预算，Chatbase 完全依靠**有机营销**（Organic Marketing）：通过在 Reddit、Twitter 和 LinkedIn 上“公开构建”（Building in Public），实现 100% 的流量自然增长。这种被迫的约束反而让他习得了构建个人品牌的能力，使得后期的付费投放效率远超同行。

在规模化阶段，他引入了**暖意主动开发**（Warm Outbound）策略。这不同于盲目的冷启动电销，而是针对那些访问过网站、注册过但未付费、或曾经付费但已流失的高意向用户。通过发送个性化的 LinkedIn 互动或建立 WhatsApp 群组，团队不仅能高效转化客户，更能深入了解其真实痛点。此外，他将 **SEO** 和 **AEO**（AI Engine Optimization: 针对 AI 搜索的优化）视为同一体系，通过在社交平台、评论网站和视频平台分发内容，确保品牌在 AI 模型训练和实时搜索时都能被有效索引。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

I didn't spend any money on marketing in the first 3 months because I didn't have money. It was just building in public, launching in subreddits, Twitter, LinkedIn. When you have the skill of organic marketing, you can build a brand around your company and the efficiency of paid marketing becomes much stronger.

To boost it more, you add warm outbound. It means you take a look at all the visitors that do sign up but don't end up subscribing, or people who stop using the product. These are the highest intent people in the world. Most of the time they're happy with that call because they see your content and they feel like they know the brand. For us, it's 80% warm. It is very high-touch and very fulfilling.

Regarding SEO and AEO: Most of these models get their information by just doing a web search. If you're able to rank for humans, you'll be able to rank also for the AIs. User generated content on Reddit, YouTube and Tik Tok are also very important because everything is getting indexed when the model is trained.

</details>

### 留存与定价：以产品迭代驱动长期增长

面对早期的用户流失（Churn），Yasser 认为任何试图隐藏取消按钮的小花招都是在浪费时间。**降低流失的核心在于产品价值的持续交付。** 他通过疯狂的出货速度（Shipping Fast）向客户传递信号：我们不仅在倾听，更在以极快的速度解决问题。

在定价策略上，他强调要勇于实验。Chatbase 的最低套餐从最初的 19 美元逐步上调至 40 美元，甚至将高端自选套餐的价格从 300 美元提升至 500 美元，但流失率并没有因此改变。他指出，最大的营收增长来自于**向高端市场迈进**（Moving upmarket）。只要产品能够解决更复杂的推理和业务问题，客户并不会在意价格的小幅波动。他建议创始人必须在旧客户的忠诚度与新业务的价值转化之间寻找平衡，最好的定价是让客户觉得获得的价值远超所支付的成本。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

The reason we have customers stick with us for a long time is because they see the product improve every day. This sends a signal that we're putting effort into making this a better product. Honestly, I think it just comes down to how good your product is and how good is it at communicating its value.

We overhauled our own onboarding flow to make sure that we surface all of these capabilities. Anything else that claimed to have a big impact on churn that was not about improving the product was probably more of a waste of time.

Regarding pricing: We started at $19 as the lowest plan, we're now at $40. We also experimented with moving the highest self-serve plan from 300 to 500. We basically didn't have any change in churn because people were seeing a lot more value. The biggest impact came from moving up market. I've never seen a company regret experimenting with their pricing, but I've seen many companies not experimenting enough.

</details>

### 决策底层逻辑：低自尊与高灵活性的平衡

在快速迭代的 AI 行业，Yasser 认为**自尊心（Ego）是公司发展的阻碍**。很多创始人因为害怕在员工面前“打脸”而固守已过时的决策。他提倡一种基于新输入的动态决策机制：如果外部环境（竞争对手、新模型发布、市场动态）发生了变化，那么两周前的正确决定在今天可能就是错误的。

他建立了一种鼓励“推翻坏决定”的企业文化，并建议屏蔽外界关于“GPT 套壳”（GPT Wrappers）的杂音。他指出，那些最初被嘲笑为套壳的公司，如今已通过构建强大的“模型管理平台”实现了三年破亿美金的 ARR。他的经验总结是：**低头构建真正有价值的东西，让外界去猜测和质疑。**

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

A lot of people have a lot of pride and ego behind the decisions they're making, especially if they make that decision publicly because you don't want to look bad in front of your employees. But I think it's actually a sign of low self-confidence. If the inputs change, you realize that this is not the best decision moving forward, but your ego is not allowing you to go back on that decision, that's a recipe for a company to fail.

The takeaway here is to just ignore the noise. People are going to speculate about GPT wrappers and how there's no future, but now GPT wrappers have rebranded to model harnesses and they're all the hype. You don't lose anything if you just put your head down and just work on building something valuable.

</details>
