### 2026年加强区域治理法案：权力版图的重组

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[John Michael McGrath]**: 好的。这项法案专门针对安大略省**区域性市政政府**（Regional Municipal Governments）的改革，这些政府全部位于安省南部，特别是我们所说的**大金马蹄地区**（Greater Golden Horseshoe）。包括多伦多周边的市镇，延伸至**尼亚加拉地区**，北至**锡姆科县**（Simcoe County）。在这些地区，存在一个高级别的区域政府，负责为下设的许多小型基层市政政府做决策。

这项法案（以及之前的相关法案）带来的两个重大变化是：首先，这些区域议会的**区域主席**（Regional Chairs）现在将由**委任产生**，而不是通过直接或间接选举产生。他们将由**皇后公园**（Queen's Park，即安省议会）、由市政事务与住房部部长直接任命。其次，区域主席还将获得类似于多伦多或渥太华等城市市长已拥有的**“强力市长”职权**（Strong Mayor Powers）。此外，锡姆科和尼亚加拉区域议会的规模也将有所调整。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[John Michael McGrath]**: Right. So this is specifically looking at reforming the regional municipal governments in Ontario, most of which all of which are in southern Ontario. And specifically, these are the regional governments that are mostly in what we would call the greater Golden Horseshoe. So municipalities around Toronto, but into Niagara region as well, up to Simcoe County. And these are municipalities where there is one big overarching level of municipal government that also makes decisions for many smaller, lower level municipal governments. And two big changes that this bill does well, this bill and previous bills do is it will now make the regional chairs of these municipal councils appointed as opposed to elected, either directly or indirectly. So they will be appointed from Queen's Park, from the Minister of Municipal Affairs and Housing, and separately, the Regional Chair will also be given powers analogous to the strong mayor powers, that mayors of other municipalities like Otto and other municipalities have received. And then on top of that, there's also some changes to the size of the regional councils in both Simcoe and Niagara.

</details>

### “强力市长”权力的具体内涵与争议

**[主持人]**: 好的。那么，“强力职权”对这些区域主席意味着什么？

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Host]**: All right. Okay. So what do strong powers mean for these regional chairs?

</details>

**[John Michael McGrath]**: 让我们先从权力本身说起，大家可能需要温习一下。法律术语称其为**“议会首脑”**（Head of Council）。它允许议会首脑在符合**省级优先事项**（Provincial Priorities）的情况下推进某些措施。例如，加快建设公共交通或建造更多住房。任何涉及这些领域或其他省级优先事项的事务，他们可以在没有议会多数支持的情况下推动通过。他们还拥有**否决权**，且这种否决权只能由议会的绝对多数（Super Majority）才能推翻。

这在一些城市已经引发了争议，因为这不符合安大略省传统的市政民主运作方式。这是一种变革，因此备受争议。但至少在之前的案例中，这些权力是由**民选市长**行使的。而新法案将同样的权力交给了**非民选、由委任产生**的区域主席。我认为这是另一个极具争议的元素。目前的保守党政府任命的一些人，比如尼亚加拉地区最近的主席 **Bob Gale**，他曾是进步保守党的候选人。他在地方政界确实有资历，我不想否认这一点，但可以肯定地说，他被任命是因为他曾是省级的候选人。选民在省级选举中曾有机会投票给他，但选民却在该选区重新选出了一名新民主党人。他在竞选中失利了，却被任命为区域主席。所以你可以看到，法案生效后，这种模式可能会重演：**选民投票拒绝的人，现在却获得了高于民选官员的权力**。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[John Michael McGrath]**: So it's let me just start with the powers themselves. Just people might need a refresher. It will allow the head of council. And that's the language in the law. It will allow the head of council to move forward certain measures that meet provincial priorities. So we think about building transit more quickly or building more housing. Anything that that intersects with those areas and other provincial priorities, they can move measures like that forward without a support of the majority of council. They can also veto measures, and those vetoes can only be overridden with a super majority of council. So this is already this has been controversial in some cities because you know it. It's not how we have traditionally done municipal democracy in Ontario. And so it's a change. And that's been controversial. But at least in those cases, until now, those powers were held by elected mayors. This new bill gives those same powers to unelected, appointed regional chairs. This is another, I think, likely to be controversial element of this, where some of the people who have been appointed by the current PC government, you know, can go back to the recent chair of Niagara Region, Bob Gale, who was a former PC candidate. He had a substantial career in local politics. I don't want to deny that he had a substantial resume there, but he was. I think it's uncontroversial to say that he was appointed because he was a former Progressive Conservative candidate. At the provincial level, voters had had an opportunity to vote for him at the provincial level. They re-elected a New Democrat in the riding. He contested instead, and then he was made this regional chair, you know, so you very well could see a position after this bill becomes law where, you know, this this kind of pattern could repeat where somebody who the voters had, in fact rejected is now given a powers superior to those of elected officials.

</details>

### 地方民主的危机：微观管理下的自治权

**[主持人]**: 好的。让我们接着聊。显然有人认为这并不民主。你在 **TVO.org** 最新的专栏中也表达了这一观点。为什么不民主呢？请为我们详细解释一下你提到的那些内容。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Host]**: All right. Well, let's pick up on that. There's obviously some argument that this isn't necessarily democratic. You share that view in your latest column on TVO.org. Why wouldn't it be? Walk us through a little bit of some of the stuff that you had mentioned, but there's more to that.

</details>

**[John Michael McGrath]**: 是的。将这些权力交给民选市长是一回事。无论你是否同意，我先陈述一下这种做法最强有力的理由：以多伦多为例，多伦多既有市长，也有由各选区选出的议员。支持赋予 **Olivia Chow**（多伦多市长）或 **Mark Sutcliffe**（渥太华市长）“强力市长”职权的理由很简单：他们是整个议会中仅有的由全市选民选出的人，他们在全市范围内拥有民意授权，可以制定全局性的政策。你可以不认同这个逻辑，但这至少是一个在民主语境下可以辩论的、站得住脚的论点。

但是，如果该职位变成由皇后公园任命，这个论点就不复存在了，完全站不住脚。这确实从根本上削弱了**地方民主**的理念。如果我们只是要让皇后公园以一种**超强的微观管理**方式来下达政策——不仅是由皇后公园制定大规则和总体优先事项，还要对市政当局说：“你不仅必须这样做，我们还要任命一个人来确保你做到位。” 那么这就产生了一个问题：**我们最初为什么要设立地方民主呢？**

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[John Michael McGrath]**: Yeah. So it is one thing to say that we are giving these powers to elected mayors, and whether you necessarily agree with this argument, let me sort of give the strong version, I think, of this argument, which is that let's take a city like Toronto, right? The city of Toronto has both a mayor and it has councils, elected councillors elected at the ward level. And the argument to give strong mayor powers to a mayor like Olivia Chow or Mark Sutcliffe in Ottawa is pretty simple. These are the only people on the entire council who are elected by the city as a whole, and they have a mandate there, and to make policy for the city as a whole. Again, you don't have to agree with that argument, but I think it is at least a fair argument, a defensible argument that people could could, you know, genuinely debate in a democratic context. That argument disappears if the position is now appointed from Queen's Park, it's not really tenable. And it really does undermine the whole idea of local democracy in the first place. If we are just going to dictate policy from Queen's Park in a super sort of micro managerial way, where it's not just Queen's Park spelling out the the sort of the broad rules of the game and general priorities, but saying to municipalities, not only do you have to do this, but we are going to appoint somebody to make sure you do. Then it really raises the question of, why do we even have local democracy in the first place?

</details>
