### 中美AI裁员潮：法律与市场抉择的碰撞

当前，一场关于AI引发的就业冲击正在全球范围内展开，而中国和美国对此采取了截然不同的法律与政策立场。在中国，法院在两起独立案件中裁定，公司不得仅仅因为AI能够胜任员工的工作而解雇他们。这一法律原则明确指出：**AI技术采纳**（AI adoption）是企业的一项战略性商业选择，而非不可预见的“情势变更”。这意味着，企业不能将技术变革的风险转嫁给员工。与此同时，美国在2026年前四个月，有7.8万至9.2万科技工作者被裁员，其中近半数明确归因于AI。然而，美国目前没有任何联邦层面的同等法律保护。这种鲜明的对比揭示了两种不同的社会经济模型如何应对AI转型带来的挑战。

<summary>Original English Source</summary>

Here's a generally wild collision happening right now. Chinese courts have ruled in two separate cases that companies cannot fire workers simply because AI can now do their job. The legal principle they established is as follows: **AI adoption** is a strategic business choice, not an unforeseeable change in circumstances. So, companies cannot shift the risk of technological change onto employees. Meanwhile, in the United States, between 78,000 and 92,000 tech workers were laid off in the first 4 months of 2026, with nearly half of those cuts explicitly attributed to AI. And there is zero equivalent legal protection. China is protecting workers during the AI transition. The USA doesn't appear to be doing the same thing. Why is that? I'm L, I have a PhD in computer science, and I analyze AI developments to understand what is actually happening beneath all of the hype. And when you look at the actual court rulings, the actual legal framework, and what's happening in Silicon Valley at the exact same moment, the contrast is generally extraordinary.

### 杭州“乔”案：AI替代下的递归式岗位流失与法律判决

为了更深入理解中国法院的立场，我们来看一起引发广泛关注的案例。杭州的一名科技工作者，化名乔（**Joe**），曾在一线AI公司担任质量保证主管。他的主要职责是验证AI生成的答案，并优化大型语言模型的输出。他每月收入约2.5万元人民币（约3600美元）。然而，在2024年，公司认为其AI系统已足够成熟，可以实现乔所在岗位的自动化。公司向乔提供了一个薪资大幅削减的替代职位，从2.5万元降至1.5万元，相当于40%的降薪。乔拒绝了这一提议，随后被公司解雇。

值得注意的是，乔的工作本身就是对AI进行检查。当AI变得足够优秀，不再需要人类检查时，它便取代了那个原本负责验证它的人。这构成了一种**递归式岗位流失**（recursive job displacement）。乔随后提起了仲裁，仲裁委员会裁定公司解雇行为违法。公司上诉至地方法院，再次败诉；又上诉至杭州市中级人民法院，依旧败诉。法院的判决理由是：“公司提出的终止劳动合同理由不属于业务紧缩或经营困难等消极情形，也不符合导致劳动合同无法继续履行的法定条件。” 这表明法院认为，企业仅仅因为购买了能替代员工工作的AI，并不能将其作为解雇员工的**不可抗力**（force majeure）理由。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

Let me show you what I mean. Start with the case that triggered all of this. A tech worker in Hangzhou, identified only as **Joe**, worked as a quality assurance supervisor at an AI company. His job was basically verifying AI-generated answers, optimizing outputs from large language models. He earned about 25,000 yuan per month, which is about 3,600 US dollars. In 2024, the company decided its AI systems had improved enough that Joe's role could be automated. They offered him a different position with a salary cut from 25,000 yuan to 15,000 yuan, which is a 40% pay cut. Joe refused. The company fired him. Just stop and think about that for a second. Joe's job was checking the AI. The AI got good enough that it didn't need somebody checking it anymore. So, the AI replaced the person whose job was verifying the AI. It's recursive job displacement. Joe filed for arbitration. The arbitration panel ruled the dismissal unlawful. The company appealed to a district court, lost again, appealed to the Hangzhou Intermediate People's Court, lost again. The court's reasoning was this, I'm quoting, "The termination grounds cited by the company did not fall under negative circumstances such as business downsizing or operational difficulties, nor did they meet the legal condition that made it impossible to continue the employment contract." So, they're basically saying, "Just because you bought an AI that can do somebody's job, it doesn't mean you get to fire them and call it **force majeure**."

</details>

### 北京“刘”案与中国劳动法：AI商业决策的风险归属

另一起案件进一步巩固了中国的法律原则。一名自2009年起担任地图数据采集员的工人，化名刘（**Liu**），其工作主要涉及手动数据录入。2024年初，他的公司转向AI驱动的数据采集技术，取消了整个部门并解雇了他。刘在北京的仲裁案中胜诉，理由与乔案相同。

这两个案件的核心法律问题是：AI驱动的岗位替代是否构成中国《劳动合同法》中“客观情况发生重大变化”？中国劳动法将“客观重大变化”定义为不可控、不可预测的事件，如自然灾害、政府政策导致的破产等，通常是超出公司控制范围的事件，而非商业决策。法院指出，公司采纳AI是主动为保持竞争力而采取的行动。通过援引AI替代作为解雇理由，公司实际上将技术迭代的风险转嫁给了员工。法院明确表示，选择购买机器人并因此替代员工，并非“天意”（act of God），而是一个商业决策。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

There is a second case that establishes the very same principle. A worker named **Liu** had been employed since 2009 as a map data collector, which is basically manual data entry. In early 2024, his company switched to AI-powered data collection, eliminated his entire division, and fired him. Liu won his arbitration case in Beijing on the exact same grounds. The legal question at the center of both of these cases is essentially does **AI-driven job replacement** count as major change in the objective circumstances under China's labor contract law? China's labor law basically defines objective major changes as uncontrollable, unpredictable events such as natural disasters, government policy causing ruin, things generally beyond a company's control, not business decisions. And the court said the company's adoption of AI was a voluntary move to stay competitive. By citing AI replacement as a grounds for dismissal, the company had effectively shifted the risks of technological iteration onto its own employees. You chose to buy the robot, the robot replacing people is not an **act of God**. This is just a Tuesday.

</details>

### 美国科技巨头的AI裁员潮及其背后的资本逻辑

与中国形成鲜明对比的是，美国科技行业正在经历一场由AI驱动的大规模裁员潮。到2026年初，全球约有7.8万至8万科技岗位被削减，其中超过四分之三发生在美国。截至四月末，已有超过9.2万科技工作者被解雇。这些裁员中约一半明确与AI或**工作流自动化**（workflow automation）相关。

例如，**甲骨文**（Oracle）在3月通过一封由“甲骨文领导层”署名的邮件裁减了约2万至3万名员工。此次裁员预计将为甲骨文节省80亿至100亿美元，这笔资金将专门用于AI数据中心的投资。用通俗的话说，公司为了购买更多的GPU而解雇了数万人。**Meta**（Facebook母公司）在4月宣布将裁员8000人，约占其员工总数的10%。公司内部指引显示，全年裁员总数可能达到20%。这些“其他投资”明确指向了AI基础设施。**Block**（Square和Cash App母公司）将其员工数量从1万人削减至6000人，CEO杰克·多西（**Jack Dorsey**）将此归因于AI带来的新能力和对人工需求的减少，将40%的裁员描绘成一次常规业务更新。**Snap**（Snapchat母公司）裁员1000人，占其员工的16%，CEO埃文·斯皮格尔（**Evan Spiegel**）同样引用了AI的快速发展，认为AI能够减少重复性工作，提高效率。**Salesforce**在9月裁减了4000个客户支持岗位，CEO马克·贝尼奥夫（**Marc Benioff**）甚至表示：“我需要更少的人头，而不是更少的职位。” 此外，**Alphabet**、**微软**（Microsoft）、**Meta**和**亚马逊**（Amazon）预计在2026年将合计投入近7000亿美元用于AI基础设施建设。简而言之，硅谷在四个月内解雇了近10万人，其中一半明确归因于AI，同时投入7000亿美元建设AI基础设施，并将此描绘为不可避免的技术进步。

### 全球AI劳务法规：透明度与自动化保护的缺失

那么，美国和欧洲现行的法律框架对此有何保护呢？令人震惊的是，美国没有联邦法律保护员工免受AI置换。尽管存在一些零散的州法律，如科罗拉多州将于2026年6月生效的**高风险AI系统法**（high-risk AI system law），加州的隐私制度，以及纽约市的AI招聘披露要求，但这些法律主要关注AI在招聘决策中的使用方式、透明度、人工监督以及知情权（即了解算法是否拒绝了你的申请）。它们并未在员工被雇佣后提供免受AI替代的保护。

欧盟的**AI法案**（AI Act）作为全球最全面的AI监管框架，将就业相关的AI归类为高风险，并要求透明度、人工监督和解释权。该法案计划于2026年全面适用。但同样，这仍是关于就业决策中的**算法公平性**（algorithmic fairness），而非提供免受自动化的保护。该法案保障个人有权不完全基于自动化处理做出决定，除非求职者要求人工重新评估。这对于AI错误拒绝求职申请的情况很有帮助，但对于公司因AI能以更低成本完成你的工作而解雇你时，它却无能为力。截至目前，除了中国，没有任何司法管辖区裁定仅仅因为采纳AI不能成为解雇的合法理由。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

Meanwhile, what legal protection exists in the United States or Europe? Here's what's generally striking to me. The United States has no federal law protecting workers from AI displacement, none. There are patchwork state laws focused on **algorithmic transparency** in hiring, such as Colorado's **high-risk AI system law** effective June 2026, California's privacy regime, New York City's AI hiring disclosure requirements, but those laws regulate how AI is used in hiring decisions, transparency, human oversight, the right to know if an algorithm rejected your application. They do not, however, protect you from being replaced once you're hired. The European Union's **AI Act**, the world's most comprehensive AI regulation framework, classifies employment-related AI as high risk and requires transparency, human oversight, and the right to explanation. It's scheduled to be fully applicable in 2026. But again, that's about **algorithmic fairness** in employment decision, not protection from automation. The act guarantees individuals the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing unless job applicants request human reevaluation, which is great if an AI wrongly rejects your job application, but still it does nothing if your company fires you because an AI can now do your job for less money. No jurisdiction outside China as of this moment has ruled that AI adoption alone cannot justify termination.

</details>

### 风险承担的法律哲学：中美劳动法框架的根本差异

这引出了一个核心问题：谁来承担技术变革的实际风险？在我看来，美国当前的法律框架基于一个简单的前提：如果技术能够更便宜地完成你的工作，那么你就出局。市场决定一切，技术变革的成本由劳动者承担。而中国法律框架则确立了一个不同的前提：如果技术能够更便宜地完成你的工作，公司选择部署这项技术，那么这项战略决策的商业风险不能转嫁给员工。一位中国律师精辟地指出：“技术进步可能是不可逆的，但它不能脱离法律框架而存在。” 这彻底颠覆了关于自动化的传统假设。

在美国模式下，甲骨文可以通过清晨6点的邮件解雇3万人以资助数据中心，这被视为资本主义的运作方式。员工承受冲击，股东获得效率收益。而在中国模式下，甲骨文购买AI是其自主做出的商业决策。如果这项商业决策导致某位员工的合同无法履行，那是甲骨文需要通过再培训、合理调岗或支付遣散费来解决的问题，而不是员工因失业而需要独自面对的问题。中国法院的判决对此非常明确，他们认为公司应优先考虑：

*   **再培训员工**（retraining workers）
*   **提供公平薪酬的合理调岗**（reasonable reassignments with fair compensation）
*   **帮助员工过渡到需要更高人工参与的高级职位**（helping employees transition to higher-level roles requiring greater human involvement）

如果公司未能或不愿这样做，那么解雇行为就是非法的。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

Let's next talk about who bears the actual risk here. Here's what I think is actually happening. The legal framework in the United States operates on a simple premise. If technology can do your job cheaper, you're gone. Market decides, the cost of technological change falls on the worker. The Chinese legal framework just established a different premise. If technology can do your job cheaper, the company chose to deploy that technology and the business risk of that strategic decision cannot be transferred onto the employee. A Chinese lawyer put it perfectly and I'm quoting here, "Technological progress may be irreversible, but it cannot exist outside a legal framework." Which flips the entire assumption about automation, doesn't it? In the US model, Oracle can fire 30,000 people via email at 6:00 a.m. to fund data centers, and that's just how capitalism works. The workers absorb the disruption, the shareholders get the efficiency gains. In the Chinese model, Oracle buying AI is a business decision Oracle made. If that business decision makes somebody's contract impossible to perform, that is Oracle's problem to solve through **retraining** or **reasonable reassignment** or severance, not the worker's problem to solve by becoming unemployed. The court rulings are very specific about this. They know that companies should prioritize retraining workers, offering reasonable reassignments with fair compensation, or helping employees transition to higher-level roles requiring greater human involvement. And if the company can't or won't do that, then the firing is illegal.

</details>

### “不合理调岗”的法律界定与结构性分歧

在杭州的乔案中，法院判决指出，向乔提供的40%降薪的替代职位，并非“合理的调岗方案”（reasonable reassignment proposal）。这意味着中国劳动法已有判例明确，公司不能以近乎减半的薪资作为调岗条件，并声称已履行了法律义务。这凸显了在AI大规模应用背景下，中美两国在处理劳动力市场转型时的**结构性分歧**（structural divergence）。当Meta以“更高效运营”为由裁员8000人，并同时投入数十亿美元购买GPU集群时，美国缺乏相应的法律机制来质疑或阻止这类行为。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

Now, there is a generally funny detail buried in the Hangzhou case. The court ruled that the alternative position offered to Joe with a 40% pay cut, if you remember that one, was not a **reasonable reassignment proposal**, which means Chinese labor law has case precedent that says you cannot cut somebody's salary nearly in half, call it reassignment, and act like you fulfilled your legal obligation. Wild that this needs to be litigated, but here we are. Meanwhile, Meta is telling 8,000 people "We need to run more efficiently" while spending billions on GPU clusters, and there is no legal mechanism in the United States that says, "Wait, just hang on a second."

</details>

### AI时代的全球竞争：市场与社会稳定的未来走向

展望未来，这种政策上的差异将带来显著的全球竞争分化。中国正在积极部署AI，预计到2025年底30%的制造商将采纳AI，到2030年普及率将超过90%。同时，中国法律要求企业承担技术转型成本。而美国也在积极部署AI，但转型成本几乎完全由劳动者承担。

那么，哪种方法能带来更稳定的劳动力市场？哪种方法能维持消费者购买力？哪种方法能避免因快速自动化导致的**大规模失业**（mass unemployment）而引发的社会动荡？答案即将揭晓。

其次，中国法院的判决将AI采纳定义为一种**商业战略**（business strategy），而非外部冲击。这一点至关重要，因为它改变了法律分类。如果AI置换被视为“天灾”，那么裁员就具有合理性。但如果它被视为任何其他资本投资决策，是企业的主动选择，那么法律责任就会改变。北京仲裁委员会明确指出，公司采纳AI的决定是自愿的，通过终止雇佣合同，公司将技术变革的成本转嫁给了员工。

第三，我们正在见证自工业化以来最大规模的劳动力置换事件的开端，而早期信号表明，中国倾向于在影响尚不明朗时保护劳动者。美国似乎则选择让市场自行调节。中国社会科学院的一位研究员指出：“这是否会降低中国公司的竞争力，或者增强其韧性，将取决于AI是真正取代工人，还是仅仅改变了工人的工作内容。” 从美国9.2万裁员，以及众多公司明确表示为资助AI而裁员的早期证据来看，美国的情况非常明确：AI正在取代工人，仅此而已。而中国则明确表示，这并非没有法律后果。未来十年进入劳动力市场的一代人，将生活在各自国家选择的法律框架下，而目前，这些框架正朝着截然相反的方向发展。

<details>
