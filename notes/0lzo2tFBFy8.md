### 软件的历史演变：从存储到行动

**[Mike]**: 给人们一个拥有无限权力的聊天框，他们却只会说“给我讲个老掉牙的笑话”。在技术世界里，未被利用的能力是巨大的。现在说模型的发展远超它们所交付的价值，几乎已经是陈词滥调了。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Mike]**: give people a chat box that can do unlimited power and they're like tell me a dad joke. In the technology world, the underutilized capabilities are so big. It's almost trit now to say the models are far ahead of the value they're delivering.

</details>

**[Alex]**: 从 1960 年到 2022 年，整个软件史就是你把一个**文件柜**变成一个**数据库**。而现在 AI 领域发生的酷事是，那个“文件柜”可以开始**干活**了。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Alex]**: The whole history of software from 1960 until 2022 was you would take a filing cabinet and you turn it into a database. The cool thing about everything that's happening in AI land is that the filing cabinet can do work.

</details>

**[Mike]**: 这种认为我会通过“**氛围编程**”（Vibe Coding）搞定自己的 **Workday** 工作流然后运行它的想法是挺吓人的。然而，我们确实在软件的可扩展性方面看到了巨大的进步，比如利用 V 编码（V Coding）这类技术。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Mike]**: The idea I would vibe code my own workday and then run it is terrifying. However, there is a great gain we are seeing internally in extensibility of software using things like V coding.

</details>

### SaaS 公司的三类划分

**[Alex]**: 我认为公开市场目前无法区分三种不同类型的 SaaS 公司。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Alex]**: Alex, how how about you? ...my kind of cursory glance is that there were three different types of SAS companies and the public markets couldn't tell the difference between the three.

</details>

**[Alex]**: 第一类是席位（Seats）与产出紧密挂钩的。比如 **Zendesk**。如果你用 AI 替代了客服，你可能根本不需要这么多席位，产出却没变。如果不改变定价模式，这类公司的收入默认会趋向零。但如果转向**基于结果的定价**，收入反而可能翻倍。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Alex]**: And one is where seats are tied to outcomes. ...category one is like you have seats, the seats are being used to produce some element of work, but now uhoh, like you don't need the seats anymore to produce the element of work. So like Zendesk would be like patient one there...

</details>

**[Alex]**: 第二类是按人头收费但与工作产出不直接挂钩的。比如 **Workday**。GE 有 34 万员工，Workday 按人头收费，这感觉很“公平”。这些员工并不是用 Workday 来产生工作结果，所以 Workday 很安全。甚至 AI 可以帮它做得更好，比如自动进行背景调查，只要它是**记录系统**（System of Record）。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Alex]**: On the complete other side of that is you might have per seat pricing because it feels fair but the seats are not tied to an outcome. So like workday has this great pricing model... those employees that work at GE are not using workday to produce an outcome. So workday I think is fine.

</details>

**[Alex]**: 第三类是介于两者之间的，比如 **Adobe**。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Alex]**: And then there are things that are in the middle like Adobe like yeah it's maybe you need more seats maybe you need fewer seats but it's not as stark...

</details>

### 定价的“可预测非理性”与公平感

**[Alex]**: 丹·艾瑞里的《**怪诞行为学**》（Predictably Irrational）里有一个锁匠的例子：一个锁匠 1 分钟开锁收 500 美元，客户会觉得被宰；如果锁匠笨手笨脚折腾了 9 小时才开锁，客户反而感激涕零多给小费。人类会为**无能**买单，因为这感觉更“公平”。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Alex]**: ...there's this great book by Dan Arieli called Predictably Irrational. ...imagine parallel universe light, you know, locksmith comes um spends nine hours trying to let you in... You're so grateful that he spent nine and a half hours helping you get into your apartment that you give him a $200 tip... humans are kind of capable and willing to pay for incompetence. Like it's like a lot of pricing is about fairness.

</details>

**[Alex]**: SaaS 的按席位计费也是一种“公平诡计”。即便增加一个数字席位的成本几乎为零，但大公司多付钱感觉上是合理的。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Alex]**: ...if you think about how we got to SAS like per seat per month... it just feels fair it's like oh you have 500 seats you pay more money than if you have one seat...

</details>

### 比较优势与软件的“隐性规则”

**[Alex]**: 至于“氛围编程”会取代一切软件的说法，我觉得很荒谬。1817 年大卫·李嘉图提出的**比较优势**理论依然适用。你可以自己种地、自己焊接铝材，但你更应该做你产出最高的事。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Alex]**: ...the undercurrent of oh I'm going to v code everything which I think is just preposterous... the person that I like to cite as my counter example here is my my second favorite economist um David Ricardo um and in 1817... this is where the theory of comparative advantage comes from.

</details>

**[Alex]**: 软件不仅仅是代码，它是数十年经验积累下来的**确定性规则**和各种边缘案例（Edge Cases）。比如印第安纳州的产假法律细节，除非你踩过坑，否则你写不出来。软件将这些隐性规则嵌入其中，不是随便能复制的。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Alex]**: ...but what happens in Indiana if the person leaves and they're on maternity leave like all these edge cases where it's just you don't know about them unless you've encountered them in the wild. So uh a lot of software is just a set of deterministic rules that have been learned from like in many cases decades of experience...

</details>

### 企业即流程：超越记录系统

**[Mike]**: 我个人讨厌“记录系统”这个词，它听起来像是个静态的死数据库。对我来说，企业是一套**流程**。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Mike]**: I personally hate the system of record thing because it sounds like, oh, a system of record is just like a database sitting there. It's very static. ...to me businesses are a set of processes. They're not a system of record.

</details>

**[Mike]**: 我有超过一万名员工每天带着大脑进出办公室，我不生产原子，我只负责**协调流程**。在知识经济时代，这就是企业的全部。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Mike]**: I have 10,000 plus people who walk into buildings every day and bring their brains and walk out and take their brains with them and that's it. ...And I am all about coordinating the sets of processes...

</details>

**[Mike]**: 我把流程分为两类：**输入受限型**和**输出受限型**。
1. **输入受限型**：如客服（Zendesk）或法务。客户的问题数量是相对固定的，你追求的是效率、成本和速度。
2. **输出受限型**：如创意、营销或**软件开发**。理论上可以做无限多的任务，受限于创造力。在这里，效率的提升会转化为更多的产出，而不是削减投入。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Mike]**: There are what I like to call input constrained and output constrained processes. The customer service example with Zendesk, that's input constraint. ...Then I have kind of output constrained work. If I think about anything creative, marketing, I would argue software development, technology, where I can theoretically do an unlimited amount of tasks, right? I'm constrained by my creativity...

</details>

### 信任、设计与 AI 的“哈利波特咒语”

**[Mike]**: 用户其实对 AI 感到恐惧，不是因为它强大，而是因为缺乏**信任**。如果 AI 悄悄发了 15 封邮件清空了你的收件箱，你会担心它到底干了什么。设计必须在不烦人的前提下，建立这种“你确定要我这么做吗”的确认机制。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Mike]**: First is customer trust is really hard in these areas, right? ...they're very scared of AI, not because of its power, because it does stuff and they're like, \"Hey, how do I know that was right and what did it do?\" ...to gain trust it has to come back to you and say here's what I'm about to do are you sure you want me to do this...

</details>

**[Mike]**: 现在的 AI 往往是“一锤子买卖”（Oneshotting）。你在网上看到一堆所谓的“魔法提示词”，像哈利波特咒语一样。但现实是需要不断迭代。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Mike]**: ...So much of AI is oneshotting things. Sit on X, you'll see a thousand like, hey, this is the magical prompt incarnation Harry Potter spell... And like that's like kind of ridiculous because the reality is you also have a lot of iteration on the data side...

</details>

**[Mike]**: 我们在 **Jira** 引入了 AI 代理。起初人们想知道它在干嘛，我们就给出了上千个执行步骤，结果用户又烦了：“你跟我说这些废话干嘛？”这就是设计的挑战：如何在提供透明度的同时保持简洁。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Mike]**: ...and so we see that we just shipped you know agents in Jira in a lot of ways so you can like assign work to an agent... when we test it with people they're like what's it doing like do you want to give us a thousand steps they're like why are you telling me all this crap?

</details>

### 协作的新范式：从空白页到 Rovo

**[Mike]**: 我们推出了 **Rovo**。传统的文档协作是：一个人对着空白页，写标题、填内容。Rovo 改变了这一点：你可以让它先去调研、生成模板。我们发现资深用户非常喜欢分屏模式：75% 是文档，25% 是聊天窗口。你可以对它下令：“把所有标题变蓝”，这在 Word 里可做不到。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Mike]**: ...With create with rovo, you can literally say start with a prompt... Teaching users that they should start that way is really, really hard. ...they now have two panes, right? They have 75% of the screen is the document itself and 25% is a chat window. ...you can write commands like you know what make every heading blue which you can't do in Word...

</details>

**[Mike]**: 这是一种范式转移。就像人们第一次看到 Excel 时会问“我在哪写段落”一样，我们需要时间让用户习惯这种与组织知识库共同创作的新方式。

<details>
<summary>Original English</summary>

**[Mike]**: ...It's a completely different paradigm to writing a simple document... Maybe the first time someone looked at Excel, they were like, \"Wait, where do I type the paragraphs... Now it's just like, \"Oh, yeah, I get Excel. I know how it works.\"

</details>
