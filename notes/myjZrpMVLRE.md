### 社交悖论：为何我们回避让自己快乐的行为？

在人类生活的核心存在着一个根本性的**社交悖论**（Social Paradox）：数十年的研究一致表明，人类是高度社会化的生物，与他人建立连接能让我们更快乐、更健康；然而在现实生活中，我们却不断错失这些连接的机会。我们回避与陌生人交谈，在公共场合退缩到自己的“孤独孤岛”中，即便交谈也往往止于肤浅的客套。这种现象引发了一个深思：如果社交连接如此有益，为什么我们表现得如此“不合群”？

这种矛盾在芝加哥大学行为科学教授 **Nicholas Epley** 的一次通勤经历中达到了顶点。在拥挤的通勤火车上，这群高度社会化的生物挤在一起，却选择在长达 45 分钟的时间里彻底无视彼此。这种“死寂”背后隐藏着深刻的心理防线。为了打破这种沉闷，Epley 决定进行一次自我实验：他主动向一位戴着亮眼红帽子的女士搭讪。尽管大脑在瞬间罗列了无数个拒绝的理由——“她不想说话”、“她会觉得你是变态”、“你们没有共同语言”——但当他开口称赞对方的帽子后，原本沉闷的旅程瞬间转化为一场充满温情的深度交流。这次经历揭示了一个关键洞察：**社交连接不是自然发生的，而是一种基于预期的主动选择。**

<summary>Original English Source</summary>

There's a fundamental paradox right at the core of human life. On the one hand, decades of research have shown that we are highly social creatures who are made happier and healthier by reaching out and connecting with other people. And yet, on the other hand, every day there are opportunities, big and small, to reach out and connect with other people that we choose not to take. We avoid talking to strangers. We lean back and type to each other rather than leaning in and talking to each other. If being socially connected is so darn good for us, then why do we so often seem to be so darn unsocial? 

This paradox hit me like a freight train one morning while I was on an actual train, commuting into my office at the University of Chicago. Everybody was in a desperate search for their own little acreage of solitude right along the window. We chose to ignore each other. A woman sat down next to me wearing a fabulous, killer, stylish red hat. I decided to put myself in an experiment. Instead of keeping to myself and doomscrolling, I'd try to have a conversation with her. The second I had that thought, my brain started screaming at me all the reasons why this was a really, really bad idea. "Clearly, she doesn't want to talk to you... She's going to think you're some kind of creep." Nevertheless, I decided the experiment must continue. I turned to her and I said, "Hi, my name is Nick. I love your hat." She turned to me, a big smile, her face all lit up. The conversation just flowed really easily. 30-minute train ride just went like that. The contrast between my beliefs about how that would go and how it actually went was pretty sizable. And there, in that gap, was a potential resolution to this paradox. Social connection, after all, isn't something that just happens to us. It's a choice we make.

### 错位悲观：一种自我实现的心理误区

通过对超过 3 万名不同国籍和年龄受访者的研究，Epley 发现这种“社交恐惧”源于一种普遍的心理倾向：**错位悲观**（Misplaced Pessimism：即系统性地低估社交互动带来的积极反馈）。在实验中，通勤者普遍预测与陌生人交谈会是一次糟糕且尴尬的体验，他们认为保持孤独会更快乐。然而，当研究人员强制要求一部分人进行互动时，结果恰恰相反：那些尝试连接的人比保持孤独的人感到显著更高的幸福感和满意度。

这种错误的认知具有**自我实现**（Self-fulfilling）的特性。如果你坚信与陌生人交谈很糟糕，你就永远不会去尝试，从而永远无法获得修正错误认知的证据。这种悲观不仅存在于浅层的闲聊中，在涉及深层话题（如感激、脆弱时刻）时更为显著。人们在面对“你生命中最感激什么？”这类深刻问题时往往感到畏惧，但一旦进入对话，互动的深度和质量往往远超预期。研究表明，我们拥有创造深度社交连接的巨大力量，但如果我们低估了对方的积极回应程度，我们就不会行使这种力量。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

In my career, my wonderful collaborators and I have now conducted well over 100 experiments with over 30,000 people and found that my tendency to be overly pessimistic is not unique to me. In one of our very first experiments, we asked commuters to predict how they would feel if they kept to themselves or if they tried to connect. People thought talking to a stranger was a really bad idea. But when we actually randomly assigned them to either keep to themselves or to try to connect, we found exactly the opposite results. People’s beliefs about social interaction here weren’t just wrong -- they were precisely backward. But notice that if you believe that talking with a stranger would suck, you wouldn't try it, and then you'd never find out that you might be wrong about that. Pessimistic beliefs in that way are self-fulfilling. 

We've now seen this tendency to be overly pessimistic over and over again. We’ve had more than 4,500 people have deep conversations with a stranger. When I show people these questions, I can feel a sense of dread spreading over the room. But when I actually put them into the experiments, the trouble I have is getting them to come back. These conversations go much better than people expect that they will. We find in our research that we have tremendous power to create meaningful social connection every day of our lives. But if we underestimate how positively our efforts to reach out and connect with someone will go, we won't use that power that we have.

</details>

### 从数据驱动的勇气到性格重塑

这种研究发现彻底重塑了 Epley 的个人生活。他提出了**数据驱动的勇气**（Data-driven Courage）这一概念，即利用科学数据来修正直觉上的悲观，从而赋予自己行动的力量。无论是在火车、飞机还是杂货店，他开始养成抬头微笑、主动问候的习惯。这种微小的选择累积起来，最终导致了性格的质变：从一个内向、顾虑重重的人转变为一个更开放、更友好的人。

这种积极的影响不仅限于言语交流，还扩展到各种**亲社会行为**（Prosocial Behavior）中。研究证实，人们在给予赞美、表达感激、寻求帮助或进行随机善举时，接受者的正面感受总是远超给予者的预期。改变并非一蹴而就，而是像“移山”一样，通过一次又一次正确的微小选择来实现。每当我们克服一次心理上的阻力去连接他人，我们就在建立一种新的生活习惯，这些习惯最终构成了我们的品格。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

No research I've ever been involved with has changed the way I live my life more than this. My train rides are almost never silent anymore. I met amazing people on planes and in cabs. Even just walking around town is more pleasant for me because I’ve made a habit of walking around with my head up smiling and saying hello to other people. When I feel grateful, I write a note and send it off. When I need help, I’m less reluctant to ask for it. It's made me a more open, friendlier person and as a result, changed all of my relationships.

These changes didn't happen to me overnight. They happened, of course, slowly, over time. Just like you move a mountain -- not by pushing it all at once, but one shovelful at a time. The way you change how you approach other people happens slowly, over time. One choice after another. One small choice, as you learn where your mistaken beliefs about other people might be holding you back needlessly. And then you develop habits that then become part of your character and part of who you are.

### 终极挑战：在生命最痛处选择连接

“数据驱动的勇气”不仅能改善日常心情，在面临人生重大抉择时同样具有决定性作用。Epley 分享了家庭的一段沉痛经历：十年前，他的妻子 Jen 在怀孕期间得知女儿 Sophie 患有唐氏综合征，随后女儿不幸在出生前夭折。在长达数月的哀悼后，Jen 提出了一个挑战性的提议：是否应该领养一名患有唐氏综合征的孩子？

面对这个选择，Epley 坦言自己最初充满了被放大千倍的悲观恐惧——“我们能应付吗？”、“我们能产生爱吗？”、“这个孩子会如何回应我们？”。然而，正是职业生涯中那成千上万个关于“低估社交快乐”的数据点给了他力量。他意识到，人类对建立深度连接后的喜悦有着本能的低估。基于这种“数据驱动的勇气”，他们前往中国领养了 Lindsey。尽管抚养残障孩子确实充满挑战（Lindsey 是“填满双臂”的重任），但她带来的爱、欢笑和生命丰盈度，远超之前任何悲观预期的想象。

最终，Epley 强调：**错位悲观并不意味着我们应该随时随地社交，而是提醒我们，我们回避社交的频率往往过高了。** 当你在犹豫是否要伸出手时，请记住科学的数据：结果往往会比你预想的好得出奇。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

Overcoming my misplaced pessimism also affected how I've made some big choices, including when pain struck my family. 10 years ago, my wife Jen and I learned that our daughter, Sophie, had Down syndrome, and three months after that, we learned that our daughter had died before she could be born. Losing our daughter was absolutely horrible. Until one morning Jen asked whether we might consider adopting a child with Down syndrome. And there it was, the choice. Do you reach out and connect? 

I had all the pessimistic fears multiplied by 100 or 1,000. My first thought was, I don't think we can do this. But my second thought then started turning to my data. I started thinking about thousands and thousands of data points of people underestimating the joys they would experience when they reach out to engage with someone. And it gave me data-driven courage. Jen and I boarded a flight to China to meet Lindsey, two years old, with big dark eyes and a relentless smile. Connecting with Lindsey has enriched and blessed our lives so far beyond what my pessimistic expectations beforehand ever possibly could have imagined. 

Connecting with other people is one of the most consistently enjoyable experiences we'll ever have. And yet, our choice is thwarted by overly pessimistic fears. Being overly pessimistic doesn't mean we should reach out all the time; it means we tend to hold ourselves back a little too often. You want to change your life for the better? I suggest keeping some data-driven courage in mind. And when in doubt, reach out.
