### 全民抵制潮：当 AI 愿景撞上现实的利益墙

一个吊诡的现象正在美国上演：人们越来越讨厌人工智能，却又无法停止使用它。根据 **盖洛普**（Gallup: 美国权威民意调查机构）的最新数据，Z 世代对 AI 的兴奋度在一年内从 36% 暴跌至 22%，而愤怒情绪则从 22% 飙升至 31%。与此同时，承认使用 **ChatGPT**（Chat Generative Pre-trained Transformer: OpenAI 开发的对话式 AI 聊天机器人）等平台的人数比例，已从 2025 年底的 48% 攀升至 2026 年 3 月的 56%。这并非典型的“技术抵制”——即早期采用者狂热而大众冷眼旁观。这是一种**全民普及与全民怨恨并存**的特殊状态。人们在点击工具的同时，深信这项技术会伤害社会、夺走饭碗，并摧毁创意工作。这种情绪崩塌是全方位的，且正在加速。

<summary>Original English Source</summary>

Here's a paradox for you. Americans increasingly hate artificial intelligence, but they can't stop using it. Gen Z excitement about AI collapsed from 36% to 22% in a single year, according to Gallup. Anger rose from 22% to 31% over the same period. Meanwhile, the number of people admitting to using platforms like ChatGPT climbed from 48% in December 2025 to 56% in March 2026. So, this is not typical tech backlash where early adopters love something and everyone else ignores it. This is mass adoption paired with mass resentment. People are using AI tools while simultaneously believing that technology will harm society, cost them their jobs, and undermine creative work. I'm El. I have a PhD in computer science, and I analyze AI developments to understand what's actually happening beneath all of this hype. And what is happening right now is a sentiment collapse that is both broad-based and accelerating. The question isn't so much whether there is AI backlash. The data confirms it's real and growing. The question is more what is driving it and what it means for where this technology goes next.

### 从兴奋到厌恶：日益扩大的价值鸿沟

数据清晰地展示了这种断崖式的趋势：相信 AI 对社会有负面影响的美国人比例稳步上升，从 2024 年底的 34% 一路飙升至 2025 年中的 47%。**皮尤研究中心**（Pew Research Center: 美国无党派事实库，研究社会、舆论及人口趋势）的调查发现，仅有 10% 的美国人对日常生活中增加 AI 使用感到“兴奋多过担忧”。相比之下，AI 在民调中的不受欢迎程度甚至超过了某些极具争议的政治机构和人物。这种反感情绪并非源于无知，而是源于深刻的**价值观鸿沟**。**斯坦福大学 2026 年 AI 指数报告**揭示了一个巨大的脱节：56% 的 AI 专家相信该技术将在未来 20 年对美国产生积极影响，但仅有 10% 的普通民众持相同观点。大众非常清楚 AI 能做什么，他们只是单纯地不喜欢它所带来的后果，特别是对深伪（Deepfakes）和人类对技术依赖程度增加的恐惧。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

Let me show you the trend because it is super stark. The share of Americans who believe AI will have a negative impact on society has steadily increased. 34% in December 2024, 40% in March 2025, 41% in April, 47% by June. Only 6% believe AI's impact will be very positive and 20% think it will be somewhat positive. When Pew Research asked Americans whether they were more excited or concerned about increased AI use in daily life, only 10% said they were more excited than concerned. An NBC poll found 46% negative sentiment towards AI, making it less popular than ICE and President Trump. Just let that sink in. Artificial intelligence is now one of the most unpopular things in America. And it's not just generalized anxiety. Specific concerns are spiking. The share of Americans very concerned about AI generated deep fakes rose from 58% to 63%. Concerns about increased human dependency on technology went from 45% to 50%. Worries about diminished creativity and drive climb from 44% to 49%. Gen Z is leading the anger. But here's what makes this backlash different from typical tech skepticism. It is informed, not ignorant. Stanford's 2026 AI index report found a massive disconnect between AI experts and the public. 56% of AI experts believe the technology will have a positive impact on the US over the next 20 years. Only 10% of the general public agrees. That doesn't sound like a knowledge gap only. It sounds more like a values gap. People understand what AI does. They just don't like it.

### 职场崩塌：被自动化的阶梯与“冗余清洗”

反 AI 情绪最直接的驱动力是**失业焦虑**。这并非杞人忧天。2025 年全美有近 5.5 万次裁员与 AI 因素直接相关。大型企业如亚马逊裁员 1.5 万人，**Salesforce**（全球领先的客户关系管理软件服务商）CEO 马克·贝尼奥夫更是直言不讳：因为 AI 已经完成了 50% 的工作，他们解雇了 4000 名客服员工。这种压力在 Z 世代身上体现得最为淋漓尽致，18 到 24 岁的年轻员工报告失业恐惧的可能性是老员工的两倍以上。更残酷的是，49% 的 Z 世代求职者认为 AI 已经贬低了他们大学学位的价值——当你花费四年时间和巨额学费练就的技能在踏入职场前就被自动化，那种挫败感是毁灭性的。所谓的**职场阶梯**正在断裂：原本作为“练手”的初级岗位正在消失，而如果没有初级岗位提供基础经验，企业未来将面临严重的资深人才短缺。尽管存在**冗余清洗**（Redundancy Washing: 企业借 AI 之名进行裁员以减轻舆论压力的行为）的质疑，但对大众而言，感知的现实就是结果。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

One of the biggest drivers of anti-AI sentiment is very straightforward. People think it's going to cost them their jobs, and they're not actually that wrong. AI was cited as a factor in nearly 55,000 layoffs in the US in 2025, according to data from Challenger Gray in Christmas. That is out of 1.17 million total layoffs, the highest level since the pandemic. Amazon announced 15,000 job cuts. Salesforce CEO Mark Benov said 4,000 customer support workers were let go because AI was already doing 50% of the work. Accenture and Lufansa cited AI in restructuring announcements, too. And worker anxiety is climbing in response. Employee concerns about job loss due to AI skyrocketed from 28% in 2024 to 40% in 2026. According to consultancy firm Mercer's global talent trends report, which surveyed 12,000 people worldwide, Gen Z workers are feeling this pressure most acutely. Surveys show that 18 to 24 year olds are 129% more likely than older workers to report fearing AI could make their jobs obsolete. And 49% of Gen Z job seekers believe AI has already diminished the value of their college education. Let's just think about what that means. You spend four years and tens of thousands of dollars on a degree and before you even enter the workforce, the skill you were trained for are being automated. Entry-level positions, the ones that teach you how to actually do the work are slowly disappearing. Fortune noted in a 2025 analysis that without junior roles providing foundational experience, organizations risk a long-term shortage of senior talent. Of course, the career ladder is slowly breaking and Gen Z is watching it happen in real time. Now, there is a debate about whether AI is actually causing these job losses or whether it's just a convenient excuse for layoffs in a tough economic climate. Otherwise, Deutsche Bank analysts suggest that AI redundancy washing, which is blaming cuts on AI to deflect criticism, will be a significant feature of 2026. Yale University's budget lab found that AI hasn't yet caused widespread job losses when you look at labor market data from 2022 to 2025. The share of workers in different jobs hasn't shifted massively since Chad GPT's debut. But here's the thing, perception matters a huge deal. After years of executive citing AI for headcount reductions, the public has just accepted the narrative. Whether AI is currently destroying jobs at scale is almost kind of beside the point. People believe it will and they're adjusting their sentiment accordingly.

### 创意权的生死战：工业级规模的“盗窃”

对于作家、艺术家和音乐家来说，AI 的威胁不是抽象的，而是**生存式的**。目前已有超过 70 起创作者起诉 AI 公司的版权侵权案件。2025 年最受关注的进展是 **Anthropic**（由 OpenAI 前成员创立的 AI 安全与研究公司，开发了 Claude 模型）在版权诉讼中达成了 15 亿美元的和解协议——这是美国历史上最大规模的版权追偿额。该公司被证实用数百万本盗版书籍训练模型，最终不得不为约 50 万部作品支付每部约 3000 美元的赔偿金。音乐界同样炮火连天，乡村歌手托尼·贾斯蒂斯起诉 **Suno** 和 **Udio**（生成式 AI 音乐平台），指控其未经授权使用录音，并生成了几乎完全相同的歌曲副本。甚至在好莱坞，2023 年长达 148 天的编剧罢工其核心也是 AI。**美国编剧工会**（WGA: 代表影视编剧利益的工会组织）最终争取到了严格限制：AI 不能被视为“编剧”，工作室在提供 AI 生成材料时必须披露，且不能利用 AI 降低人类创作者的报酬。创作者们的核心控诉一致且有力：AI 公司在未征得许可、未支付报酬的情况下，利用他人的版权数据训练出可以生成类似内容的模型，这在从业者看来就是**工业级规模的盗窃**。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

Next up, we have to talk about the creative industry. If you're a writer, artist, or musician, the AI backlash is not abstract at all. It is existential. There are right now over 70 copyright infringement lawsuits filed by creators against AI companies. The biggest development of 2025 was Anthropic's $ 1.5 billion settlement in the BART's case, the largest copyright recovery in US history. Anthropic had downloaded millions of pirated books to train its models. And when the evidence became undeniable, they settled for approximately $3,000 per work, covering roughly 500,000 titles. Musicians are suing AI music generators. Country artist Tony Justice filed complaints against Suno and Udio, alleging the companies used his sound recordings without authorization and that their models reproduced exact or near exact replicas of his songs. Universal Music Group settled its lawsuit against UIO and established a licensing deal with an opt-in structure for artists. Visual artists are in court over AI image generators trained on their work without permission or compensation. and writers. The 2023 writer guilt of America strike, one of Hollywood's longest at 148 days, was fundamentally about AI. The central issue was whether studios could use AI to generate or rewrite scripts without fair compensation to human screenwriters. The agreement the WGA reached includes specific restrictions. AI cannot be considered a writer. Studios cannot use AI to write or adapt scripts without notice to and compensation for the human writers involved. Any AI general material provided to writers must be disclosed and cannot be treated as source material that reduces human creators credits or compensation. The underlying grievance across all these cases is essentially the same. AI companies took copyrighted data, used it to train models that can now generate similar content, and didn't ask permission or offer compensation from the perspective of working creators that is theft at industrial scale.

### 物理世界的冲突：基础设施与民意堡垒

抵制运动不仅停留在意识形态层面，它正演变为**物理层面的阵地战**。AI 的运行需要庞大的物理基础设施，而美国各地的城镇正纷纷拒绝在其家园建设数据中心。目前已有 69 个辖区颁布了数据中心建设禁令或暂缓令，而一年前这个数字仅为 8 个。拒绝的理由非常具体且沉重：数据中心会耗尽水资源、推高当地电价并绕过民主程序。在部分地区，随着公用事业供应商升级基础设施以满足数据中心的巨大需求，批发电价在五年内暴涨了 267%，而这些成本往往被平均摊派到每个普通消费者的账单上。密歇根州的萨琳镇投票否决了 **OpenAI** 和 **Oracle** 价值 160 亿美元的“星际之门”（Stargate）数据中心项目，尽管开发商随后起诉并强制动工，但这让当地居民感到极度的被剥夺感。佛罗里达州州长罗恩·德桑蒂斯签署法律，要求数据中心全额承担其自身的电力和基建成本，他在签署仪式上称 AI 为“面向消费者的垃圾（Slop）”，并强调世界上最富有的公司不应让佛州民众为其运营提供补贴。这种**成本转嫁**的动态，正让社区意识到他们在为巨头的利润买单，而这种不公平感正在引发强烈的政治反弹。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

But you know, anti- AI sentiment isn't just ideological. It is also physical, playing out in towns and cities across the USA that are rejecting the infrastructure AI requires to function. According to a US data center moratorium tracker, 69 jurisdictions have now enacted bans or moratoriums on new data center construction. A year ago, that number was eight. Four of these bans are permanent. And the reasons are very concrete. Data centers massively increase electricity costs for residents, consume enormous amounts of water, and often bypass local democratic processes. Wholesale electricity prices have skyrocketed by up to 267% in some areas over the past 5 years as utility providers upgrade infrastructure to handle data center demand. But the costs are being passed equally to data centers and average consumers resulting in higher utility bills for everyone. In Michigan, Saline Township voted down a $16 billion OpenAI Oracle Stargate data center. The planning commission rejected it. The township board voted 4 to one against it. Two days later, the developer sued for exclusionary zoning. The township settled and construction began anyway. Residents who opposed the project felt steamrolled. One local resident told Fortune, I'm quoting, "I feel like I'm playing by a different rule book, like I'm playing baseball and they're playing football." In Florida, Governor Ron DeSantis signed a law requiring data centers to cover the full cost of their own electricity and infrastructure instead of shifting those expenses to residential ratepayers. At the bill signing, DeSantis called AI consumer-facing slop and said the most wealthy companies in the history of the world shouldn't have individual Floridians subsidizing their operations. Between April and June 2025, 20 proposed data center projects worth a combined 98 billion dollars were blocked or delayed due to local resistance. Communities are realizing they're being asked to bear the cost. Higher electricity bills, water consumption, environmental impact while tech companies reap the profits and they're essentially saying no.

</details>

### 未来博弈：被强加的变革与民主的反击

为何情绪崩塌得如此之快？简单来说，人们对 AI 的**生活体验**与他们被告知的**美好承诺**正发生剧烈偏离。当初的宣传是 AI 将增强人类工作、提高生产力并释放创造力；而现实感受到的却是 AI 合理化的裁员、飞涨的电费以及版权被窃取后生成的廉价替代品。公众并不是因为“不懂技术”而反对，相反，他们是基于截然不同的评估标准。专家谈论的是基准测试和能力上限，而公众关心的是工作、成本、孩子的教育价值以及艺术家能否生存。更深层的是一种**民主缺失感**：当社区投票反对数据中心却被强行推进，当编剧罢工保护生计却被 AI 公司持续“喂养”作品，这听起来不再是“拒绝创新”，而是“拒绝被掌握金钱和政治权力的行动者强加意志”。目前的抵制并非过眼云烟，趋势线显示情绪仍在恶化而非稳定。最新的调查显示，82% 的美国人不相信科技巨头能够自我监管，72% 的人希望加强行业监管。当 AI 的“传教士”们坚持认为抵制只是对未知的恐惧时，也许现实是人们已经看清了利益的底牌，并认定这笔交易并不划算。

<details>
<summary>Original English Source</summary>

So what is happening here? Why is sentiment collapsing so fast? I think the simple answer is basically people's lived experience with AI is diverging sharply from the promises they were sold. The pitch was that AI would be a tool to augment human work, make us more productive, unlock creativity. The reality people are experiencing is job costs justified by AI, utility bills rising to subsidize data centers, creative work devalued because models trained on stolen content can generate knockoffs at scale. And critically, this is not people don't understand the technology. Stanford's AI index report shows the opposite. There is a massive gap between AI insiders who are optimistic and everyone else who are not. And it's not because the public lacks information. It is because they're evaluating AI based on different criteria. AI experts tend to think about capabilities, benchmarks, what the technology can actually do. The public thinks about jobs, costs, whether their kids education will be worth anything, whether artists can still make a living. And on those metrics, AI is failing to deliver benefits that justify the current costs. The backlash also reflects something much deeper, a sense that tech companies are bypassing democratic processes, enforcing this technology and people whether they like it or not. Michigan residents voted no on a data center. Construction started anyway. Writers went on a strike to protect their livelihoods. AI companies kept training on their work. Communities said, "We don't want to subsidize your electricity costs." tech companies sued or lobbied until they got what they wanted. This doesn't sound like resistance to innovation. That's resistance to having decisions imposed by actors with vastly more money and political power. Well, where does this go from here? The anti-AI sentiment we're seeing isn't a temporary backlash that will fade once people get used to it. The trend lines are going in the wrong direction for that narrative. Sentiment is worsening, not really stabilizing. The gap between insiders and the public is widening and not closing. And the backlash is manifesting in concrete ways. Lawsuits, strikes, moratoriums, legislation. This isn't just people complaining online. It is organized resistance with real consequences for how and where AI gets deployed. A recent survey found that 73% of Americans support requiring companies to implement safety measures and mandatory pre-deployment approval from a government board for advanced AI models. 72% want more regulation of the AI industry. 82% don't trust tech company executives to self-regulate. The public has basically decided they want AI controlled, not unleashed. Now whether that actually happens is of course a different question. Tech companies are spending record amounts to protect their interests and back industry friendly candidates. Billions are being poured into AI infrastructure whether communities like it or not. But the backlash is real. It's growing and it's based on legitimate grievances. Cost shifting, job displacement, copyright infringement, environmental impact, and the sense that this technology is being forced on people by actors who don't care about the consequences for ordinary workers and communities. AI evangelists keep insisting that resistance is just fear of the unknown, that people will come around once they see the benefits. But what if people have seen the current benefits, weigh them against the cost, and decided the trade-off just isn't worth it for them?
