### 准确率幻象与慕尼黑法院的破局裁决

在之前关于 Google AI 搜索的视频中，我曾指出：**Google 的 AI 概览**（AI Overviews）具有 91% 的准确率。这个数字听起来很让人安心，直到你把它代入每年 5 万亿次的搜索量中——剩下的 9% 意味着每小时产生约 5700 万个错误答案。这就好比如果商业航空只有 91% 的成功率，那就不叫航空业，而是必须购买人寿保险的极限运动。近期，德国慕尼黑地区法院的一项裁决证实了这种担忧：法院裁定 Google 的 AI 概览不是传统的搜索结果，也不是第三方内容的摘要或链接，而是 **Google 自己的语言**。因此，Google 必须为这些 AI 生成的虚假声明承担直接责任。

在这起案件中，慕尼黑的两家出版商发现，当人们搜索他们的公司名称时，AI 概览错误地将他们与诈骗、订阅陷阱和可疑的商业行为联系起来。AI 甚至编造了一整套包含危险信号和防骗提示的虚假叙事。法院的裁决明确指出，AI 用自己的话语和逻辑结构重写并判断了这些结果，而其底层的来源链接中根本不存在这些声明。更关键的是，法院驳回了 Google 以“用户可以通过点击链接自行核实”为由的辩护。在这个只有 1% 的用户会点击 AI 概览来源链接的时代，**通过进一步研究来证伪某种说法的可能性，并不能免除发布者的法律责任**。这项裁决打破了过去二十年来保护科技平台的“避风港”逻辑——你不能既运行着一台自动制造谣言的巨型印钞机，又声称自己只是个送报纸的跑腿者。

### 密西西比法院闹剧：数字妄想的系统性反噬

除了搜索引擎自身生成内容的争议，在使用 AI 工具的专业领域，缺乏人工验证同样带来了灾难性后果。不仅是德国，就在同一周，美国密西西比州的一名联邦法官发现，在一起法庭案件中，双方律师都使用 AI 来准备他们的法庭文件，并且**双方都引用了根本不存在的案例**。这位法官果断取消了整个庭审。这是法律史上第一次因为“数字妄想的双重鸡尾酒”而推迟审判。在这个荒诞的案例中，两台人工智能以每秒 60 个 token 的速度虚构出不存在的法律判例，而四名通过了司法考试的律师却充当了美化版的“复制粘贴实习生”。

建立起对滥用 AI 的心理防线后，具体的行业博弈现状也随之显现：律师使用 AI 本身并不是问题——它是一个出色的工具，可以用于加速研究、构建初始结构或探索新的视角。然而，**你绝不能将 AI 视为人类核心工作的替代品**。在当前的进化阶段，AI 就像一个能力极强但心智尚浅的 10 岁儿童，你不能把庭审案卷交给它然后撒手不管。这个事件揭示了一个深刻的教训：AI 虽然能识别模式并快速输出，但它不具备**判断力**（Judgment: 对复杂现实进行交叉验证与决策的能力）。作为专业人士，核实来源、查阅所引用的案例并确认其真实性，从来都不是可选项，而是职业的核心定义。将 AI 作为增强工具能够极大地提升生产力，但如果用它来替代思考，结果就是让人类成为虚假证据的被动旁观者。

### RAG 架构下的技术盲区与生成时失控

要真正理解这些法院裁决为何具有历史性意义，我们需要深入探究 AI 搜索在技术底层的运作机制。Google 的 AI 概览并非预先写好并储存在数据库中，而是使用一种称为 **检索增强生成**（Retrieval-Augmented Generation, RAG: 通过检索外部信息库来增强语言模型生成质量的技术） 的架构，在每次查询时实时生成。这就意味着，每一次 AI 概览的呈现，都经历了“搜索相关网页”与“大语言模型实时综合”的瞬间过程。在短短 1.4 到 5 秒内，模型（如 **Gemini**）会读取多个来源，用自己的话综合出一个连贯的回复。这个过程中没有母带，没有编辑审核，更没有人类在发布前阅读过它。这就好比一家餐厅的主厨直到客人坐下才决定菜单，凭感觉把食材扔进搅拌机，如果客人因此中毒，餐厅老板却需要承担法律责任。

从计算机科学的角度来看，这种实时的不可预测性暴露出三个严峻的失败模式：
* **检索失败（Retrieval failure）**：系统提取了错误的文档或混淆了名称相似的实体（这正是慕尼黑案的根源）。它本质上是一个命名实体消歧（named entity disambiguation）问题。
* **合成失败（Synthesis failure）**：模型面前有正确的来源，但做出了来源不支持的声明。AI 正在进行“虚构”，目前针对“忠实度验证（faithfulness verification）”的研究正在推进，但这会增加延迟和计算成本，不符合现有产品的极速设计。
* **信心校准失败（Confidence calibration failure）**：模型将不确定的信息与确定信息以同等权威的视觉效果呈现出来。尽管学术界对“不确定性量化（uncertainty quantification）”已有深入研究，但科技行业为了维持“无所不知”的产品形象，通常拒绝在产品中承认其不确定性。

这三大技术挑战表明，更可靠的 AI 内容生成路径并不神秘，技术方法一直存在，只是在“速度与能力压倒一切”的市场逻辑下，它们从未被列为首要任务。

I want to take a step back now and explain something technical because it changes how you understand the scale of the German rulings implication. Google's AI overviews are not pre-written. They are not stored in a database waiting to be shown when somebody searches for something. They are generated in real time per query using a technique called retrieval augmented generation or rag. Here's what this means in plain language. When you type a search query, Google system does two things in sequence. First, it searches its existing index, so the same database that powers traditional search results, and pulls the top relevant web pages for your query. Then, instead of showing you those pages as a list of links, it feeds the text from those pages into its Gemini AI model and asks the model to write a summary. The AI reads the sources, synthesizes an answer in its own words, generates citation links, and serves the result to you. The entire process takes between 1.4 and 5 seconds. For complex queries, it can pull from four to eight sources simultaneously and weave them into a single coherent response. So, every single AI overview is a unique piece of content that did not exist until somebody searched for it. It is manufactured and consumed in the same moment. If you search the same query tomorrow, the AI might generate a slightly different response because the underlying sources may have changed or because the model's generation process involves a degree of randomness. There is no master copy. There is no editorial review. There is no human being who reads the output before it reaches you. It is written, served and read in a single unbroken sequence. It is pure unadultered automated jazz except instead of musical notes, it's improvising corporate liabilities at scale. Now think about what the Munich court has said. These are Google's own words and Google is liable for them. At Google's scale, billions of queries per day. The court is effectively requiring quality assurance on content that doesn't exist until the instant is delivered. Imagine running a restaurant where the chef doesn't decide the menu until the customer sits down, randomly throws ingredients into a blender based on a vibe, and you, the owner, are legally liable if it poisons them. That is essentially Google search engine right now. It is a realtime culinary roulette, except the soup is made of algorithmic hallucinations. That is, with current architecture, a problem of genuinely extraordinary computational difficulty. You cannot prevet a statement that hasn't been written yet. You cannot fact check content that is generated at the moment of serving for now. For context, moderating user generated content on a social media platform, which is already an enormous challenge, by the way, involves reviewing content that exists before publication. AI overviews do not exist before publication. They exist only at the moment of delivery. The court has demanded accountability for a product whose output is by design unpredictable in advance. And this is where my background in computer science becomes very relevant because the ruling effectively identifies a research problem. One that the field has been aware of but has not prioritized with the urgency it deserves. The failure modes in AI generated search responses break down into three broad categories and understanding them matters because each one requires a different kind of solution. The first one is retrieval failure. The system pulls the wrong documents or confuses entities with similar names. This is exactly what happened in the Munich case. The AI retrieved information about genuinely problematic companies and attributed it to the plaintiffs who had no connection to them whatsoever. This is fundamentally a named entity disambiguation problem which is a deeply boring academic way of basically saying the AI looked at two different companies and basically went ah they both start with a capital letter. It's basically the same thing. The solutions exist, knowledge graphs, entity linking, verification against structured databases, but they have not been deployed at the speed and scale that Google's real time generation demands. The second category of failure is synthesis failure. The model has the right sources in front of it, but draws connections or makes claims that are not supported by those sources. This is the 56% ungrounded sources problem I cited in the Google video before. More than half of AI overview answers that were technically correct cited sources that did not actually contain the information presented. The AI is confabulating, generating plausible sounding content that has no basis in the material it was given. Research on faithfulness verification, which is essentially a second pass that checks whether the generated text is actually entailed by the source documents, exists and is advancing. But it does add computational cost and latency. That means it's going to make it slower. That the current product architecture is not designed to accommodate. The third category of failure is confidence calibration failure. The model presents uncertain information with the same visual authority as certain information. Giving the user no signal about when it might be wrong. Everything looks equally confident, equally definitive, equally trustworthy. Research on uncertainty quantification flagging claims where the model's confidence is low or where sources conflict has been progressing in academia. There is a lot of papers on this but has not been deployed at scale because frankly it makes the product look less impressive. An AI overview that says I'm not really sure about this is a less compelling product than one that says yes this company is known for dubious business practices. I'm 100% sure the incentive to appear confident has until now outweighed the incentive to be accurate. The tech industry built these models to mimic the absolute worst traits of a tech bro. Being completely wrong, but delivering it with the unshakable confidence of a condescending jerk who owns a crypto startup. It is the digital embodiment of a man explaining your own PhD topic to you at a party. These are serious structural problems that I just described. Ultimately, they are for serious researchers to sit down with and work through methodically. It is out of scope of a YouTube video to solve them. But naming them matters because it shows that the path to more reliable AI generated content is not mysterious. The technical approaches exist. They are simply not being prioritized because the market has rewarded speed and capability over accuracy and reliability.

### 全球监管收网与 AI 的确定性未来

面对这些根深蒂固的技术缺陷，接连不断的法律裁决反而成为了推动 AI 向善的关键力量。过去两年来，大语言模型领域的发展一直遵循着特定的轨迹：绝大多数的研究精力、竞争压力和投资都集中在“企业实用性”上，让模型更擅长编码、处理结构化任务。相比之下，用于综合大众知识的**准确性（Accuracy）**和可靠性却一直退居二线，因为市场只奖励能力，而从不严惩不准确。但如今，随着慕尼黑裁决的出台、欧盟《人工智能法案》（EU AI Act）即将于 2026 年生效，以及中国发布首个关于 AI 幻觉的法院裁决，全球几大主要经济体的法律系统正在同步为 AI 生成内容划定边界。**如果一家公司必须为它的 AI 说了什么承担直接法律责任，那么“准确性”就不再是一个可有可无的特性，而是一项硬性法律要求。**

人类确实需要这项技术。随着各学科知识体量的爆炸式增长，我们迫切需要一种能够从数百万个来源中准确可靠地检索、组合和验证信息的工具。只有具备了可靠性，AI 才能真正改变教育、医疗、科学研究和政策制定。过去那种“先发布，后道歉”的粗暴策略正在走向终结。当问责制真正降临硅谷，打造具备极高准确度的 AI 系统的商业动机，将首次变得与追求速度和成本效益一样强烈。这并不是为了阻碍技术发展，而是将 AI 引向正确的方向——那些被迫做到准确的下一代系统，将证明我们今天所经历的混乱是值得的。
