Today is about the rise of gamification. why people are becoming sick of it, the business of gamified apps, and what will replace it in the AI economy. Let's dive in. This may come as a surprise, but gamification existed for a century before anyone came up with that name. The core principle is taken from basic human psychology and reinforcement principles. If you reward people with something, with tokens, for example, they tend to perform desired behavior. One of the oldest examples of gamification that history remembers till this day is the green stamps in 1896. SNH green stamps were a legendary loyalty program. Customers would get stamps for everyday purchases that could be redeemed for products from an SNH catalog. By the 1960s, SNH were issuing three times as many stamps as the US Postal Service. The stamps were not worthless because a full book of stamps could buy you real merchandise. The peculiar part though is that the tokens themselves became emotionally valuable long before they added up to anything meaningful. People chose supermarkets based on the number of stamps they would get and families devoted entire kitchen drawers to those booklets. The behavior wasn't really driven by the return on investment. It was driven by the psychology of progress and the anticipation of eventually cashing in a stack of points. Almost a century later, American Airlines launched a advantage, which became the first frequent flyer program in the United States. The program replaced paper coupons with miles that were tracked through a reservation system. The next year, Holiday End followed with the first hotel loyalty program. Within five years, a boom in points and miles programs took airlines, hotels, and car rentals by storm. These programs proved that people don't necessarily need a physical reward to sign up or to buy. The motivation can occur with non-angable points, points that can be touched or don't have any nominal value, but still drive loyalty. This behavior migrated to tech and the last step towards the epidemic of gamification became as you might have guessed the rise of online gaming and the introduction of leaderboards, streaks, points, social status and this is where our story of gamification and software begins. Microsoft came up with Xbox achievements and gamer score in 2005. When they did that, every game on the platform contributed to a single score that would work across multiple games, visible to all players. This single action elevated the concept of rewards to meta rewards that would exist outside any individual game. This introduction also altered gaming behavior because it drove users to explore more, do side quests, replay games on harder difficulties. Xbox achievements were arguably the first mass market proof that imaginary badges with zero monetary value could reshape the behavior of millions of people. In 2010s, gamification started leaving the borders of gaming and migrated into social media. Now, this might tell you how old I am, but I remember the days of Foursquare. For the younger demographic on the channel, Foursquare was a social media app where you could earn points for checking into certain places, gyms, restaurants, cafes, coffee shops, and you would be labeled according to the places you visit. For example, a gym rat or a coffee coner. Then came the legendary game called Farmville. And then after Farmville, the Silicon Valley began showing interest in gamification startups. The gamification market grew 200% in 2 years between 2010 and 12. Deote cited gamification as one of its top 10 technology trends of 2012. Then came the first university level gamification course at Wharton. Numerous online courses across various platforms. And this is how in the span of 24 months gamification went from a strange word to having a dedicated spot in Gartner's hype cycle. It became a $40 million startup category and the default prescription by consulting firms. If your product was struggling, add points, add streaks, and add leaderboards. Trigger, action, variable reward, and investment. The loop is drawn from gambling psychology. Variable rewards create dopamine-driven anticipation. This is the reason why I consider gamification phenomenon quite fascinating because of how basic it is and because of how people figured out what to do with it. By 2014, gamification had evolved from a gaming term into a full-blown bucket of features for enterprises. Deote used badges to gamify executive training and time to certification dropped 50%. Salesforce gamified sales pipelines with achievement tracking. LinkedIn introduced profile completion and badges for completing courses. Now, there has been a wave of general sentiment around fatigue from gamification across various apps. And to understand how we got to the point when we're sick of it, let's go back to how and why it happened in the first place. There are three factors that made the rise of gamification inevitable. Number one, smartphones. Before the rise of smartphones, game mechanics required a console or a computer. The games, no matter how well engineered, required you to be physically present at your desk. Smartphones, on the other hand, made the reward behavior available 24/7. Number two, data infrastructure. Modern apps can measure everything. session length, retention, churn, lifetime value, and gamification gave product teams and technology companies specific levers to pull against those metrics. And number three, the attention economy. As apps multiplied, as smartphones became more ingrained into our hands, retention became harder. Gamification was the cheapest possible way to hold your attention without building new expensive features that would require lots of research and engineering resources. The reason why the reward behavior worked in the gaming industry is because in a game the relationship between effort and reward is very transparent and predictable. Unlike a slot machine, for example, a gamer knows that the more they play, the more points or coins they will collect. What do you need the points for? To upgrade a character, for example. What's going to happen when you upgrade a character? You get a better status in the game. Does it mean anything in real life? No, it doesn't. But it has a meaning in the game. Now, apply this to fitness apps. For example, you stick to a meal plan for 30 days in a row. You collect a bunch of points to unlock premium version for 5 days, let's say, and then you get a badge at the end of the month that you can put up on a wall of achievements. And the phenomenon that's been confirmed across decades of research in psychology and neuroscience is that the largest dopamine surge occurs during the anticipation of a reward. Green stamps proved back in the 60s before any of these psychological mechanisms had formal names that stacking rewards, stacking anticipation, collecting illusions and unresolved tension changed the purchasing behavior of 80% of American households. Every gamification system ever since, from frequent flyers to dual lingo streaks, is a descendant of that standbook. So why did it collapse? The psychological tricks that sparked the initial excitement became the very thing that burned users out. Gamification that was supposed to be a fun element that would make the product less boring and more organic to use and help you learn things by playing became a commodity. During the wave of fascination and wild growth of gamified apps, Gartner made a prediction in late 2012 that 80% of gamified apps would fail by 2014. And the reason for it was poor application design. This is very similar to the AI bubble that we're witnessing right now and the video we made on why most AI startups are bad businesses. Because you can only get so far when pursuing trends and not having a solid product. Remember how I said that gamification was inevitable? Smartphones, data, and attention economy. These three conditions that made it inevitable are the very conditions that also made it unsustainable. When the iPhone came out in 2007, the App Store had about 500 apps. By 2015, Apple and Google each hosted around 1.7 million, but the number of apps people actually used was around 27. And that number plateaued even as time spent on phones kept climbing. Which means that apps were competing for 27 slots on a person's iPhone. This environment made gamification the cheapest available tool for retaining users. Points, badges, leaderboards, streaks cost next to nothing to implement compared to building genuine product differentiation. And the problem is it works in the short term, but still it works. Introducing gamification as a patch fix was rational for businesses. Speaking from experience, after building a career in product management, sometimes you have to find the cheapest possible way to fix a problem. But this approach comes at a cost. for gamification. It got bolted onto apps as a retention hack rather than building a business with a defensible value that won't be wiped away by hype cycles. And what's even worse is that it disfigured the notion of games because games are not about points or coins or badges. These things are as far from games as they are from fitness or language learning. Streaks are the most effective retention mechanic in modern gamification. And they also have a destructive second order effect. They convert genuine interest into anxiety. Even Dualingo admits this. Long streaks motivate through loss aversion rather than positive anticipation. What this means in playing English is that the streak doesn't make users want to learn more. It means that they're afraid to stop and therefore they end up doing the absolute minimum to simply preserve the streak. But preserving the streak has nothing to do with learning. The streak is there, but you're learning essentially nothing. The gamification of learning in mobile apps has replaced the product's actual value. Forsquare is the clearest business case of gamification fatigue. The app had 45 million users in 2013, and all of that audience was built on check-in badges and mayorship competitions. But by 2013, the novelty of checking in wore off, and the gamification could not sustain the interest because the value of the product outside of gamification simply wasn't there. So, how come Dolingo didn't follow the same path? Dolingo is a notable exception because they invested over 600 experiments to get gamification mechanics to perfection and essentially outrun the trademill. I can have whatever opinions I want about Dolingo's learning mechanics, but I got to give it to them. They managed to build a stellar system for discovering and testing behavioral signals faster than any other competitor. In behavioral economics and psychology, there is a term called hedonic adaptation. And Dolingo manages it beautifully. It constantly refreshes visuals, rules, rewards, streak freezes so that the brain never fully habituates to the game mechanic. They make their app a living system. On top of it, they built a very defensible technical infrastructure. They tracked micro behaviors across hundreds of millions of users. Most gabified products back in 2010s never invested at this depth. And even now, compared to today's market, Dolingo's product culture is rare. Yes, they built their business on points and badges, but they also built the data science and experimentation plumbing to understand why it worked or failed. Now, from the language learning app standpoint, and some of you may disagree, but I personally do not see them as a product that has anything to do with linguistics, but that is a subjective opinion. Nevertheless, Dolingo is a notable exception. Most companies don't have the resources or the culture to sustain that level of experimentation, which is why the Gartner 80% failure rate proved accurate for the broader market, but not for them. Now, where is gamification headed in the AI economy? Despite all odds, the gamification market is projected to grow from $20 billion to $190 billion by 2034. But the term called gamification eight years from now will bear no resemblance to the points and badges system that we see today. AI will personalize the hell out of it to the point when there is going to be a built-in behavioral layer inside each app. So what exactly will happen? Number one, personalization is going to be on the user level. Social media, gaming, e-commerce will be reading behavioral signals and know exactly what motivates each individual user. When they're about to disengage, how to change the experience on the spot, what to put in your notifications, how to adapt lesson difficulty, how to change your training plan depending on how you're feeling today. What AI will do is mimic the system that Dolingo pioneered. AI will turn lots of products into living systems that will constantly change and adapt. Number two, listen closely. The definition of loyalty will be reframed. Latest research and predictions around transactional loyalty. Transactional loyalty being discounts, points, and cash back show that there is going to be a strong trend towards emotional loyalty. Things like recognition, relevance, and relationship with the product. So to all founders, techies, and users, of course, gamification isn't dead. Yes, we're absolutely bored of it at this point, but it's about to change fundamentally. So, say what you will about Gen Z trends, but vibes are becoming the factor when it comes to loyalty.
