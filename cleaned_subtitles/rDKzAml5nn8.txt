So, utopia? Well, hold on. Let's make the case against an AI government. I've always said that if I can't argue something from both sides or all sides, I don't understand it well enough. So, let's also explore the case against an AI government. Because with great power comes also a great potential for dystopia. First of all, who programs this AI? Behind every AI is a programmer or more accurately a massive team of engineers, data scientists, ethicists, and let's be honest, corporate stakeholders with vested interests. AI doesn't exist in a vacuum. It reflects the priorities, assumptions, and blind spots of the people and systems that built it. If the model is trained on biased data or poorly defined objectives, the outputs will reflect that bias even if they seem neutral. And that's the dangerous part. A biased human is obvious. A biased algorithm may look cold, crisp, and objective while quietly reinforcing inequality. Garbage in, garbage out. Just with a much fancier user interface and a veneer of credibility. Then, separately, it doesn't feel. It doesn't have empathy. Real governance isn't just about cost efficiency or logical consistency. It's about values, lived experience, and human connection. Should we deport somebody because an algorithm calculated a 74.2% likelihood of failed asylum claim? Should we slash funding for elderly care in the north just because it's suboptimal for the national budget? Should we not provide welfare for disabled people just because the AI decided they can't contribute to the economy? Of course not. The most critical decisions often involve compassion, not calculus. An AI won't pause for a moment of silence. It won't feel moral outrage at injustice. It won't see a parent's grief or hear the trembling voices of a scared child. Sometimes the humane choice is irrational and AI doesn't do irrational. Next and most likely the most dangerous one of all is the lack of accountability. If a human leader fails us, we can protest, vote them out, hold inquiries. There's a name, a face, a sense of public responsibility. But if a policy is made by a machine, whom do we blame? The developers? The institution that deployed it? The algorithm itself? Ourselves? Even if we could assign blame, how do you protect an opaque model running on a cloud server in another country? You can't just chant down with the neural nets on the steps of parliament and expect change. And finally, let's address the black box problem. This is a pretty standard problem in AI machine learning. Many AI systems, especially deep learning models, are inscrutable even to their creators. They can make stunningly accurate predictions, but we often don't fully understand how they got there. So what happens when we hand over decisions about schools, hospitals, or justice to a model that we can't fully interpret? Why did the AI shut down 50 schools in the Midlands? We're not really sure.
